Si. Why me, and not you?

How smooth he was, the way he gave me the bad news, like it was nothing, a bump in the road, not the end of me. ‘Never despair,’ he says, with no smile, a dead face. He said the words tough, not soft like a woman, because women care more. If I could be there when his time comes, to see him kick and hear him scream, begging for one more day, one more hour, one more minute, I would say, ‘never despair, dutturi. There are things we can do.’

A month of worthless treatments, but I pay because I do not want to die. All I ask, when the times comes, give me one more day. Sempre domani. Tomorrow is always better than today, but today I am sicker than yesterday (the bill comes Friday with breakfast, under the plate of eggs). I refuse to complain, or be the annoying man, as I have always hated annoying people and complainers and men not content with what they have, not satisfied with the way things are. Somewhere in the heavens I was crossed with a mule, of this I am certain, but I have never told it that way, for fear of insulting mama and papa. It was a joke, nothing more, but I admit being well trained by the circumstances of my life. Drag it over there and drag it back and keep your goddamned mouth shut. Are you complaining, cavone? I will slap that scowl off your face. Be strong. Never complain. It is strength, and the back breaking labor that makes you happy. It is not necessary to believe what you are trained to believe; but never complain. Just do it.

These pills they serve me, all colors; green, red and blue and white. I take them, no questions, because in a while, I will float, like a feather. They say you get used to this feeling and like it. Women like it. I did not at first, but I have learned to like it. A yellow pill to sleep, a white pill to wake up and the red pill that’s helps me piss in the tube, and a blue one, so I do not piss too much in the tube.

I asked one day, though not complaining. “Is there a pill for the sweats…at night?”

“Night sweats? Certainly. Just ask,” they say. “Why haven’t you asked…that’s why we are here.”

The green pill works, when it works, dries me out and cracks my skin and my mouth bleeds when I eat. Is there a pill for nightmares? I take what they offer, no questions asked, just rid me of this little bastard who bumps my bed in the night and speaks to me, scaring me to death, but I have never seen him, only the whites of his fucking eyes dancing around a black room.

“Antonino Sparacino(he calls me by name). I am waiting patiently,” this little bastard says, then he makes a wicked laugh. “But not forever. I will not wait forever. You are a selfish man, Antonino. I have others to see; better men than you. I do not have all night to do my.” He laughs and I cower like a beat dog, sweating like a pig. “Women, too,” he says, laughing. “I never forget the women, but children are the best, as they cause the greatest grief and pain for the greatest number, and this is what I am about.”

Then he disappears, andato, he is gone. What is his name, can you tell me this, and why am I freezing when the room is like a furnace? Where is the sense in it?

I have been here before, a few years back, Carmela says, and she is never wrong.

This same doctor, this cavone, took out a kidney he said was “diseased,” no good anymore. “It has to go, but do not despair.” He lifted a finger to the front of his nose, “You only need one kidney. I will fix this, and you will be fine, I guarantee it. Trust me.” When it was over, how pleased he was with the fine work he did that day. So pleased, he said it again. “You only need one, that’s all, and the one you have left is perfect. You will live to be a hundred years old…a hundred years old.”

A hundred years. ‘Centu anni’ is how you say it where I come from.

A hundred years, he says, but I am failing. Who makes good the thirty years I am owed?

The nights are long, and I fear them, like a child. In the morning it is not so bad, there is good light from the window, and there are people, and food; two eggs and bread (American bread) and something they swear is oatmeal, but it is not bad if I bury it in salt. The nurses clean me, like a mother cleans a baby, but for me it is not a good thing; it shames me, like I have committed some crime. If the hose leaked during the night, they change the bedding and I am surprised they smile, but how can they like it, or do they hate me for it.

I pass the daylight hours staring at the Irish Lace hanging from the ceiling light and the breeze through the open door makes them flutter and dance around. The lace is longer every day and I hope to be here when they fall from their own weight. Watching dust is how I pass the day.

Where is he? He knows I want to see him, but I will understand if he does not come. What young man wants to smell the stink of death in the air. There is nothing here, nothing new, but old life. There is new life in Memorial, down the street, but not here.

I paid no attention when I hear the door open, figuring it to be some nurse with a pill, or a needle, and she will say, “it won’t hurt at all.” Do I need this shit, I ask Carmela, who prays to *Sant’Agata, and she tells me Sant’ Agata needs time to arrange a good miracle? Sant’Agata goes to Santo Pietro, and Santo Pietro sits at the right hand of God, with His ear. This takes time, she says. “Maybe if you prayed with me, but I have never seen you pray.” I shake my head.

“Pray? I have no use for it because I am an honest man.” She smiles.

“You are a good man,” she says, “but you would be a better man is you prayed with me

She comes here every day, this beautiful woman, and I wonder, how can I be a sinner, if Carmela, who is a saint herself, has been with me all these years? She stays until I am asleep and when I wake, she is gone, but now I feel a stirring at the side of my bed.

“Nonno? Are you awake? Its Michael.”

Michele? Not Carmela?

He has come to see me, like I asked, or do I dream it, or is everything a dream? I force a look to my right. It is Michele, like he says. When I fell off, I was talking to Carmela, I think, and watching the lace, and she was watching a bird on the sill flapping its wings in the snow.

I envy him. A bird, a free bird not caged.

“Michele, mio nipotu. Come in. Tu siedi. Sit down.”

I am glad he is here, but surprised, he tells his father he is afraid he will throw up on the floor and cause me embarrassment. Barely in the door, already his face is grey, like mine, but I am still happy he is here.

“Vienne Qua,” I say. He smiles.

“Papa said you wanted to see me. I was coming Sunday after church, but papa said I should come today...are you allright? Papa said you…”

“Grazie, Michele. Si. I am glad you came.”

“I almost missed the bus. How are you feeling today? Papa said…?”

“I feel good, Michele. Better than yesterday. Grazie for asking.”

The truth, my pain is like I imagine the pain of hell to be if there is a hell. My legs are swollen, numb from knees to the floor. I piss through a god damned tube somewhere near the coglioni. He is sixteen years old; a boy in America, but a man in Sicilia, who would do a man’s work to survive and feed a family. Maybe he is seventeen. Si, seventeen. Certo. I am sure of it. He is already taller than his father, Orazio, my oldest (he is forty-three, as I recall, or forty-two, but Carmela will know). He is the first grandchild on both sides, and I promised my son I would be there when he is born. It was a long walk to the bus stop from the yards; two transfers, but I made it there like I said. His other grandfather, a good man, but a lesser man (not Sicilianu) was not there or was his wife there. They come up at night when it was over, and his wife, the mother of the mother, believed she deserved a special place, and said as much when she made her entrance. Do this, do that, she says nasty to the nurses, and the big nurse says, she would throw us all out if the mother interfered again.

But I was there, on time, like a good watch, and when he was born, I was the first grandfather he saw. *Gino Moro, a good neighbor, come to find me at the engine house, and said they brought my daughter-in-law to the hospital. The buses were slow and crowded and hot, like hell. I prayed some in those days, and I prayed I would be there before he was born, to welcome him (I knew he would be a boy, I would have it no other way). I was early by a good ten minutes, and on his part, this was his show of respect for his grandfather, that he waited for me before leaving a good place for this place. When I see him for the first time, he was all Sicilian eyes, and I think to myself, how beautiful he is, and they will call him Antonino, after me, and I will watch him grow and help him along the way, if I am able, and things will be better for him and they will not run him out like they run me out, those bastards. Was I insulted when my daughter-in-law named him Michael (not even Michele), after her father, *Michele Petrulli, who likes to call himself Big Mike, as if he is Americano, or Irlandese? I refuse and will not call him “Michael,” as it is Irish and the boy is not Irish, Sicilianu and some Calibraise. But no Irish. My son apologized for this ‘slight’ he calls it, blaming his wife; she did not like the name ‘Antonino,’ as it is too foreign for an American man. The truth is she does not like me(her people are Calabrese and I made jokes about them, that I now regret, but the Calabrese have always been an easy laugh. I swallowed her shit and suffered this disrespect in silence, and I smile when men ask why he is Michael and not Antonino. I say, “there are already too many Antonino’s in the world.” Friends laugh at my joke, out of respect for me, but I will wear this shame on my sleeve, forever.

Everyone knows my son to be a fine man, but he has no coglioni.

On the good holidays, Christmas, Easter, on his birthday, I take Michele aside and slip him a five-dollar bill; more than I give the rest, but he the oldest and deserves this respect, no matter he carries another man’s name. He changes by the day and seems more a man each time I see him. He has my brother’s eyes, *Salvatore, the boy I left behind to die, because I am weak, and a man who cannot tell the truth. My memory of him is pain, that I deserve. He was a boy cursed from birth, born with the gentlest nature, different from the cavones who call themselves ‘men.’ He was quiet and sensitive, respectful of all living things, and I understand that in this world these qualities are best in a woman. He was persecuted by men who think men should be violent and respectful of nothing. They laughed at him, beat him when they could, with the least excuse, and felt proud of it, and bragged to the village they were better men.

When the Great War came, they said he must fight, and do his duty to God and the King, but he refused, asking why he should kill men he did not know, who had not hurt him or his family. He was hated, because he refused to fight, while the rest obeyed, like sheep. The army sent four armed men to run down this frail and sickly boy. I was not a witness to this infamia, thank God, because I was in America, but men whose word I trust said he went hard, kicking and screaming, vowing not to fight at the whim of other men. They jailed him with the rats and vermin, for months, to wear him down. The guards beat him for no reason, cowards who saved themselves from the front because they would torture the defenseless without remorse(the best torturers I have been told). In the end they broke him, of course. He surrendered. Did he have a choice? Was he a free man? His war ended the day he escaped the trench in a canvas bag, a single shot having done him in, or a well-placed mortar round, or a bayonet; these are the stories, and we are free to choose which ever liked the best. They threw him onto a pile with other men who met the same fate, and sooner or later, the enemy would fire a barrage where the corpses were heaped, to be certain they were all dead, not pretending to be dead, as cowardly men were known to do. Salvatore is buried somewhere in the North, or maybe near the sea, and I believe my mother and father never saw his grave, or have I. The story of his death came in a letter to the priest, who brought it to my family, and my mother wept and cursed, and my father stared at the wall. When I received the letter from *Patri Carmine, our priest in Sicilia, did I cry? I did not cry, but more, I refused to cry. I have not cried to this day. Patri was a priest, a man of God, but also a common man, like all the rest. Patri said *Salvatore a hero, said I should come back home, to Sicilia, for his memorial, when he would be honored for his sacrifice. I did not go home because there was no money for passage, and the big boss said I would have no job when I returned. What would I say to my family? To Papa? I hear the bullshit about ‘hero,’ but I prayed he was no hero, that he did not die to save the willing, eager men who were there because the capu said the fight was right, and their life had no value beyond war. A Captain, or some other important man, who knew nothing of Salvatore, sent a letter because it was his job and he was paid to do it, as Salvatore was paid to die.

“Your son, Salvatore Sparacino, died bravely in battle, while advancing on the enemy,

defending his family and Country and King. It is my honor to call him one of “I cadutti,”

the fallen. His name will be carved on a marble wall in the piazza, as a tribute to the

heroes of your village, and the bells of the Church will toll nine times in his honor.”

I say this to these bastards, who love their guns and grenades and mortars, and worship the good fight, and what they call bravery and honor, and the men who write the letters, and build walls of honor in the piazzas, that when I prayed, I pray you all rot in hell.

I blame them, all these bastards, but I blame myself.

He was a boy when I left Sicilia, maybe ten, eleven. but old enough to be angry our family was in a bad way, and that I was leaving. I begged him, walk with me to the top of the mountain road, but he turned away. If we embraced that day, I have no memory of it, but I do not believe we did. A month to the day before I left, I picked firewood with papa in the woods belonging to *signuri Basile. Papa was quiet, no jokes, none of the stories he always told, or did he bring his small flask of wine, that he always carried, like a priest carries his crucifix. Papa took me by the arm, and we faced each other; his voice was weak, not thundering like always. I had never seen him so nervous.

“These woods are near played out,” I said, to break the silence; he nodded, and broke off a small branch from a dead tree, throwing it aside. He was looking at the ground, as he cleared his throat, then stared me straight in the eye, never flinching, or looking away., but he was nervous, as I could see and sweating like a work mule.

“Take your brother away from here…bring him to America.”

He waited, all nervous like an impatient man, but I said nothing.

He leaned closer to me. “What do you say?” I cleared my throat, and I suppose it meant to him that I would not bring Salvatore with me. He seemed angry.

“He will not go with me, papa. He says I am abandoning the family.”

“He will do what I say. But never tell your mother I asked. Capisce?”

“Si. Capisco. But Santo and Luca will not have him along…I have asked. It is too dangerous to bring a boy, they say.” Papa stared into the black woods. He shook his head, hard, to show his disgust with me, like many times before. He spit in the bush and turns to me and stared with his fiery green eyes.

“Never trust your life to weak men, like Luca. He is a selfish, with no feeling. Little better than a criminal himself. But Santo is family. I expected better from him.” He spit again. “I was hoping. Maybe if you went with better men?” He sighed and kicked a piece of half rotted wood into the ditch. “Never mind what I say. I am getting old. I forget that most men never do any good. When I dream at night, I dream that the worst will happen if you leave Salvatore behind. What is here for him? You know him and what he will be. His life will be short with too much pain.” I thought about what he said.

What can happen to my brother? Nothing. He is young and strong. In a few years, he will be a man; his own man. I am certain of it. Papa tried to bully me with his own dreams. He pointed his finger at my face, and I backed away. “But I will tell you this, you will regret the day you left him behind.” That was the last said of it that night. I picked as much wood as I could carry and fetched the piece papa threw in the ditch, but it was not enough, and that night, water in the tin cup was frozen solid in the morning, and it never happened before. The truth, I did not want the bother of a boy, not even my brother, my own flesh and blood. Even a lesser man would have done more for a dog, but in the back of my mind I was coming back as soon as I saved money to buy my own land, and we would all be landowners and maybe capu’s. I said this so many times, I believed it to be true. But there was the part of me who did not want to come back. What could be worse than disease and famine and dirty water, no work and the rotten bosses. I hoped I loved America. This was really my plan, not to come home, but to call for my family and they would come, but I knew mama and papa would never leave, and my brother Paolo would do what suits him, and when my sisters married, their men would make the decision, but I would ask papa to send Salvatore and I would meet him at the boat, and we would be brothers again. Some years later came the Great War, and they took my brothers, Paolo and Salvatore into the army. Paolo drove a wagon, with horses, behind the lines in Francia, suffering not a scratch and he said “it was not so bad,” but Salvatore was murdered dead by the Austrians, or Germans, or the Russians, on a wasteland with no name. Am I to blame he died? Did I fire the weapon that put him down, or load the shell into the tube, that ripped him apart after he was dead? The priest preaches about heaven and hell, the other side. If there is such a place and we meet, will my brother say I handed his executioner the sword?

Mama and papa visit me in the night and remind me of the evil I did to Salvatore.

They are the fine people I loved when they were alive, and now I love them more and respect their memory, but not the visits in the night and the terror they bring. But what do you say to the dead? They are thin and weak, far worse than the day I left home, but out of respect I say how good they look. Mama sits at the edge of my bed, rubbing my forehead., and smiles when I ask, is she wearing a new dress? “No,” she replies, “it is old like me.” I hug her hard, but wishing they would leave and not come back.

“You are my favorite,” mama says, “but never tell your brothers and sisters.”

I take her hand, twisted from arthritis and old age.

“You were a fine mother,” I say, and she would kiss my check.

“How are they treating you?” she always asks. “If you die…it is not so bad.”

Papa worries on the bill. “How will you pay?” he wants to know. “I soldi is everything with these bastards. Watch them, like an eagle, because they will cheat you if they can,” and he repeats the many times he was cheated in his life, who cheated him, and how he tried to cheat them back, but he was too good a man.

I tell mama and papa about my children, proudly naming them in the order they were born. Mama laughs, says I am a better man than most. I say Carmela, my wife, is in good health. I tell them because they will not ask. They like her, yes, she is the is mother to their grandchildren, but not blood. Every night they come I make the same mistake, and say my oldest grandchild, Michele, favors Salvatore in his fine looks. Mama cries, she curses the men who murdered Salvatore. Then she turns on me, with fire eyes.

“And you, you said you would come back.” She curses and I weep like a child. “When I was sick, you never came home.” I take her hand, but she pulls away.

“I was here, in America, thousands of miles away. What could I do?”

“I was dying…I begged you to come. I prayed, but you ignored me…your own mother.”

I raise my voice. “I did not know you were dying until you were dead and buried.”

Papa slaps my face, hard. “Show respect for your mother,” he says. “I demand it.” He steps closer to me and looks straight into my eyes; I try to look away, but he grabs my face with both hands.

“Never think I forget you left your brother to die. I know you lied to me.” He reaches for his hat from the edge of my bed. “It is time to leave,” he says, staring off at the bare wall, and I believe it is because he cannot stand to look at me, his oldest son.

Mama hugs me, kisses my forehead.

“What is tomorrow?” papa asks. “Is it giovedi? Si, giovedi…we come back tomorrow.”

Mama shakes her head. “Not on giovedi. Giovedi is market day.”

“Si. Giovedi is market day,” he says. “Maybe venerdi….si, venerdi. The next day.”

They walk into the darkness of my room and disappear.

Always after they leave, I remember the letters.

The night storm was over. It came from the north and worse than most storms; drifts four, five feet high, six feet in the driveway, but I had no car, I never learned to drive, so what was the difference? I never understood the purpose of snow, and I thought many times about what God means by a storm and why does He send it? Me, I suffer enough for my sins without it. Our mailman, *Signuri Henry Bannon was late, like always, but when I say it, he shows at the front door, complaining the sidewalk is not shoveled; the neighborhood no longer shows him this courtesy, like the old days. “Tell them they should write in English,” he said. “English.” I shake my head.

“Sicilianu. “It is Sicilianu. My language.” He nodded, though I am certain it meant nothing to him. Signuri Bannon was not an old man, but he acted like an old man, and I am not certain he enjoyed the company of immigrants to “his” country, always swearing he had nothing against “you people.”

I get a letter from Sicilia, maybe every six or seven months, but none so far this year. It has been years since two letters in one year, and never two on the same day, but I was glad to have them, even if the news is bad and the news is always bad. The last letter announced the death of my cousin *Giacomo’s daughter, “who died a horrible death from some godforsaken disease that spread through the village, like wind.” Ten dead, near ten, but she was the youngest, born after I left for America. That letter asked we pray for the people in the village, and maybe send a few lire, as times were bad, and not getting better. “Come in, Signuri Martin, and get warm.”

“Call me ‘Hank,’” he always says.

‘Hank?’ I would not call a dog ‘Hank.’

He shook his coat and a bushel of snow fell to the floor, melting fast, leaving puddles. I throw down rags and pushed the water around with my foot, hoping it would dry before Carmela came home.

“Carmela is cleaning the Church, or we both would catch hell for this mess,” I say.

He moved close and whispered, though the house was empty except for the two of us.

“Say, Mr. Sparacino, do you have a little wine?” he asks, like a bold man. “I need to thaw out…the cold…the drifts. I am not young man anymore.” He laughed and slapped my back. “You know what I mean.” He drinks two full glasses of my good wine, not my best wine I saved for family, but decent wine, aged in the old barrel for, maybe, two months. Not the best. He brings the glass to his nose and takes a deep breath and smiles, as if enjoying the smell of a good cigar, or a flower, like a red rose. I love the word “roses,” and the sound when I say it.

“I see nothing. Non vedo nulla” is what we say in the “old country.” I learned to call Sicilia “the old country.” Americani, they feel better about us when we do. “The old country.” I know now I begged a lot back and then and today I am ashamed for it.

There was a gold cross on each of the envelopes, in the corner, and I knew the letters come from Patri Carmine, who always wrote the same thing; “the Church is empty, the village is deserted. When are coming home?” I am always surprised he was still alive and a priest, after all these years.

*Our reader, Francesco Longo, was a good friend. I say ‘was’ because he is gone, morte, some years ago, but I think about him often; he was like my brother, and my family remembers him this way, and his memory is respected in my home. He lived next door and when needed he read letters and wrote letters, when we needed letters written. He could write in Italiano, Siciliano, and English; an educated man, like my friend, the teacher, *Enzo Baneri(long since gone). Francesco worked with me for years, and he enjoyed his wine (the americani on the railroad called him ‘a drunk’), but sober he had no accent when he spoke English; drunk he spoke Sicilianu, as he thought English and Italian, were the language of barbarians. Si. He was an educated man, schooled in Sicilia, in Palermu, but he ended up a nobody, like me, swinging a pick for a few cents an hour. “Ciao Antonino. Your mother, *Giovanna Faraone, is gravely ill.

Mama was dead and buried a month when the letters came.

My worry was papa, now alone, without his wife of fifty years. Six months later, in Agustu, he was in the Piazza with *Signuri Marzini, his friend of sixty years, who brought his best wine for the night, and two crystal glasses, a gift from his wife, who was dead from dirty water and the fever. The two friends sat on a wooden bench made by Signuri Marzini’s father years before, drinking wine and watching a good game of briscola, listening to the men lie about the women they had known.

Papa asked, could he have more wine, half a glass, and his friend, Signuri Marzini poured him a glass and another for himself. Salutѐ,” they said together, raising their glasses to the bell tower of the Chiesa di Sant’Agata. They tell me papas his face went white, like chalk; he moaned and turned away and held his chest, falling gracefully to the wet cobblestones; always a man of dignity, even in death. Signuri Marzini panicked, and went to papa, but papa refused his hand, as he believed a man should not ask for help, and it was too late.

“It will pass,” papa said, apologizing, like a gracious man, for the wine he spilt, and Signuri Marzini’s fine crystal glass that shattered on the stones. Papa lay there and said a prayer, and the women cried, and everyone said he was a good man. He died where he fell, in the place his father preached to the contadini, who would listen unless there was a game of briscola to watch, or the weather was bad, and they all went home. Papa was buried next to mama in their plot on the hillside; a small piece of ground that was all they ever owned.
