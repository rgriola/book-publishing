“Arrivaderci a tutti,” he said, quietly, trying to hide his emotion, but we saw it all, and he was embarrassed. Behind the bar, near hidden by thick brush, was our road to the sea.

Old man *Fama, who was ninety, maybe more was riding into the hills, on the back of his mangy mule that struggled up the slight grade behind us, once losing its footing, nearly throwing the old man. Not a farmer or landowner, by birth, but a follower of the sea, that was his calling. But to be honest, he had poor luck with the sea, as bad construction, by incompetents, he said, but mostly pirates, accounted for three boats sinking from under his feet. He survived these catastrophes by hanging onto the wreckage, the last time for over a week, when finally the Germans took him out of the water, and he was near out of his mind, and he swore to them he saw the Blessed Mother in the waves, and on hearing this, four German sailors made the sign of the cross, so maybe they were Catholic Germans and not barbarians, as most of the Germans are. The Blessed Mother told Enrico he would be saved, but it was the only time she would come to his rescue, reminding him of his poor luck with the sea, and he remembers that it was at the exact moment of her apparition, he began to love the land, vowing to become a farmer, like our ancestors. He is the oldest in the Famiglia di Ponto, and their leader for that reason, but built his farm in Termini Imerese, near Palermu, miles away. The weather is better near Palermu, he says, hotter, more humid, and grape vines grow faster and taller and give better fruit that makes better wine. A few times each year he visits the village of his birth, to see family and renew friendships. He tells stories through the night, or until the wine runs out. He is a worldly man, they say, and this is his reputation, though he has never sailed outside the Mediterranean and was sunk in it three times.

Since Zio Enrico give Luca the gun, I respect it as the right thing to do.

How do I calm Santos ragged nerves? I looked at him straight on.

“Then you are going back?” He said nothing. “Listen to me and remember what I say. Today may be your last chance out.” He stared at the ground, shuffled his feet, and the sea wind blew loose dirt toward the mountain. “What can I say to you? The gun is for our protection.

From Zio Enrico Ponto. You know him. He is alright, not a foolish man.” Santo nodded, like a child. I took him by the arm. “You have heard stories, about the Carabinieri stopping the boat from leaving Sicilia. They want to keep the contadini here until all the crops are picked. If you go home over some old pistol, you may be a prisoner there, maybe forever.” He shrugged his shoulders and curled his lip, that looked to be swollen.

“They cannot stop us all,” he said, thinking like a child.

“They will not stop us. You need a boat to leave Sicilia...you need a boat to get to America. They will stop the boats and that will stop us. The end. Finito. Capisce, il mio amico?”

Santo glances toward Luca, “What about him?”

I lower my voice. “Leave Luca to me…I will control him.” When I said it, that I would control Luca, it was my honest aim to try, but I was certain it could not be done easy, if done at all. He respected me, but would he listen to me.

“Swear it.” Santo said. “Swear to God.” I raise my right hand.

“I swear to God we will get to America in one piece, and no one will stretch the neck of my favorite cugino, even if he deserves it.” Santo nods and smiles. I put my hand on his shoulder, and now I believed everything would be all right with him, no matter how foolish he could be over meaningless slights.

“Think about this, in two years, maybe three… at the most…if things go right, we will have enough money to buy a little land in Sicilia. Is this not worth some risk? A little?”

“Our own land,” he sighed, his eyes wide, like a child at his first festa.

“What a good thought, Antonino. No more capu,”

“Si. No more capu.”

I was feeling what he felt, and I know Luca felt it, but he would not admit it, because being content is weakness to him. Our families? We leave them behind at their worst, their weakest, when we should stay to help them survive. One year…two years…three years…forever? But we hate this life, working other men’s land, the taxes, the disease. We love and cherish our family and would die for them, yet we are leaving them to chase some dream. A fool’s dream, some say. Children. Old people. The weak. We leave them behind and tell ourselves we save them by leaving them.

Santo held on to the sleeve of my jacket. “I will go on. What choice do I have? I was born with no choice. How can I go back after a few hours? What would I say to my wife? That Luca scared me. She will think I am a coward, and everyone else will say it behind my back. Everyone will agree.”

The two of them, Luca and Santo stood face to face, like two men should stand when they have disagreed, and my cousin Santo spoke first, with a ‘solemn promise’ he said, one he could not ever keep.

“If you hurt us in any way” he says directly to Luca, then he turns to me, “And Antonino, you are my witness…if I see that goddamnable pistol again …I will take it and you will never see it again.”

Why Luca did not laugh I never knew, but he stood quiet, almost a gentleman.

“Then we go!” I said, loud and passionate, like Garibaldi would say it. “Forget this god damned Fiorini and his god damned fish. We will make it to America…but there is one thing that bothers me.”

“The briganti?” Santo asked. The bandits?

“No. Not the briganti. But our packs? Are they big enough to carry the lire we bring back home?” Santo did not understand the joke, as he counted the haversacks, one, two, three.

He caught it, finally and we all laughed, and I thought that peace would be restored. Luca walked to the edge of the shade, under the tree where we stood, and stared at the sea. “What a beautiful sight,” he said, “and quiet and peaceful,” though the waves hit the beach hard making an ungodly roar. “Quiet and peaceful,” he said again. I never heard him speak so gently, like a woman would speak, about pleasant things and beautiful things, but this did not change my mind, that he must be watched and kept in a straight line, before it all falls apart and we will be the fools for it. My father said this, if you had a problem with another man, ‘speak to him like a Dutch uncle.’ I believe he meant ‘firm and forceful,’ but kind and caring, yet he never said it that way and I did not care to ask.

“Santo is afraid of you,” I said to Luca, who thought it was correct.

“But without good cause,” he said, as he pushed me away, and said other things about Santo, not kind things, about his manhood, that I would never repeat. Santo is softer than most men, but nothing should be made of his gentle nature. My brother Salvatore had a gentle nature and he died for something he did not understand.

“Santo is afraid of you…the pistol and the reckless things you have been known to do.”

“The pistol…” Luca stuttered. “The…the…the pistol… is for my protection,” he paused. “And for you, my friend. And him, too. We are looking into the lion’s mouth.”

“Will you behave yourself?” I asked him straight out, hoping for the right answer. “No going crazy if someone looks at you the wrong way.”

He raised his right arm in the air. “No fights, nothing…my right hand to God.” I was glad he said it, but it is said so often, what does it mean? I have said. “My right hand to God,” many times and it is just something you say and never mean it, but Santo says it and means it.

“Shake hands, the both of you,” I demanded it and they did it, in a way. Neither looked the other in the eye, so it was not a sincere shake, and neither seemed to feel right about, but I accepted it, as I did not want Santo to leave; I would have promised him my first born. If he left, then we were a sinking ship.

“Andiamo,” I said, “we go...”

“Si, we go,” they said it together, at the same time.

Across the road, six men, young and fit men, had showed and were waiting at the train stop, and I figured they heard the train; we took our bags and joined them, making us nine in all. Four of the men carried packs, like us, and I guessed they were heading for America, too. We waited for the train like Sicilians do, with patience. We wait, but never think about waiting.

An old man, maybe eighty years old, pulling a stubborn goat on a long rope, wandered to the middle of our crowd, and carefully lowered himself, grunting all the way, until his ass touched the ground. The goat pulled hard at the rope, and this angered the old man, who cursed for a minute straight.

“Buonjiorno,” I said, tipping my cap out of respect for his age.

He ignored me, like I was nothing and kept battling with his mangy old goat.

We waited, listening for the whistle to blow.
