She picked up her suitcase and walked slowly to the desk, where the clerk stamped her ticket and pointed to the gangplank, and she asked what time the boat would leave. She thanked him, then apologized that she fell apart and showed her weakness, but she was a long way from home and afraid. Before leaving she thanked Enzo for his kind advice, promising she would pray for him, and hoped they would meet on the boat. She glanced at me and smiled, but otherwise I was ignored. She nodded to the clerk and left quietly. Enzo shook his head as he watched her walk through the door.

“Good luck,” he said to her. She turned and smiled, then waved and walked toward the wharf, dragging her suitcase along the cobblestone path. Enzo shook his head once more.

“Poor thing. She walked from Colleberardi. A hundred miles away. She is a child.”

“She came alone.” I ask.

“Si. Her father demanded she marry a man more than sixty years old. She refused, so he drank and beat her in the street in front of the neighbors, for her disrespect. She would rather die, than marry this vile old man, so she said, but she agreed only to make the beating stop. But, as the days went on and it was close to the wedding, she feigned illness. Enough time for her brother in America to send money for a ticket. She ran away when the rest were working in the fields. Her youngest brother, *Carmine, I believe she said, followed her two miles down the road, pulling at her arm, crying, begging her not to leave. She promised to return some day and take him to America, but she knows it will not happen. Her father would never allow it. She has very little money. Only a few lire. The walk was longer than she figured, and there was food to buy food along the way. It was her good luck when she found a family on the road to Napoli, and they allowed her to travel along, until two of their children became ill and they turned back, and she was alone the rest of the way. She is not much more than a child, but she thought of turning back, and now she is a woman and alone. I said to her, this might be your last chance. If she did not take it, they would find her and force her to marry this old man. You will shape your future by the decisions you make. Of course, she is afraid. A young woman all alone. I promised to look out for her, and I believe it helped to make her right decision.”

She was a young thing, not more than a hundred pounds, maybe. A hundred and ten at the most. Fair, very fair skin, and I do not believe she worked the fields. How long does it take a woman to walk a hundred miles? Four, five days, six days. And not be waylaid, or worse, by the god damn briganti. She is a lucky young girl, as I could see, and fine looking, like my sister *Maria. Their hair was equal and they both had the same manner when they talked and walked. Is that why I am drawn to her? I asked Enzo, did he know her name? He scratched at his chin and looked off.

“Si, she did tell me. *Mascolli is her father’s name. She is Assunta Mascolli, from Colleberardi, a hill town in Frosinone.” He scratched his chin. “Frosinone. I have been in Frosinone; a poor town, itself, but I do not know Colleberardi. Her family lives in a stable with goats and all the family works the fields, but she is a house servant, and she caught the fancy of the landowner.”

I wished I could help her, as for some reason I felt a kinship to her, that maybe we had something in common, but she was too young for me and then, what about Carmela? Carmela was waiting for me.

When Assunta disappeared, we figured she found her way to the boat.

The D’arrico’s were nowhere to be seen, and this worried me; Emil’s last words, he promised to catch up. A pain hit my gut. It is my nature to believe the worst will always happen. Maybe they decided to go home, or worse, they were waylaid. I asked the clerk if he saw the family.

“I pay little attention,” he said. “So many come through here every day.

I described them as a, “a short and slim man, two children, a boy, a young girl and a beautiful woman.”

He smiled. “Ah, si. The beautiful woman. Of course, I remember her. Am I dead?” He laughed. “She was here…with a little man, like you say.” He leaned back in the tall chair. “They stood where you are and argued, then left. I wondered what she is doing with the little man; a strange match, as you probably know.” I hid my disappointment and made light of it. “They will be fine,” I told Enzo, who shrugged. He asks, “who will be fine?” We collected our bags, our papers, our tickets, and started for the boat, that Enzo named il grenbo, the womb.” When I see her close up, even the rust and dents and creased iron, she was still grand, like a strong woman and I see why ships are her, not he. My heart beat hard and fast; I was lightheaded, and sick in the stomach. I lied to the rest, saying there was a stone in my boot, and I hobble to a bench, refusing help, though Santo wanted to stay with me, but I waved him away. I take off my boot and pour this imaginary stone to the ground, rubbing my foot vigorously, in case they were watching my little act. In a few minutes my heart right and the sweats were gone, maybe cooled by the breeze. When I dropped my bag and stooped to pick it up, through the bushes I see Emil on a bench, staring at the black sky and talking to himself. I called out his name; he looked around, seeming to be confused. Amaria was next to him, her daughters’ hand in her hand, and the boy, Giovanni, sitting and his feet were swinging in the breeze. Not a care in the world. I pushed through the bushes and walked to them and stood in front of the bench; Emil saw me and looked through me but did not speak.

“Emil, what is wrong? Are you not going to the boat…are you…are you sick?” He looked away, as I figured he was embarrassed to be sick, like a woman. A man is never sick at times like this. Never.

“Are you going back?” I asked. “Is that it?”

His face was red and swollen, as if he had been punched a thousand times.

“Home? We have no home,” he said.

Amaria cleared her throat and waited a respectful time. “We have no tickets,” she said. “It is my fault for what I did.”

I stare at Emil, not believing what I heard.

“Is this true?” my friend.

“We are bad off,” he said. “Bad off.”

“But no tickets? But how is this possible? I saw your tickets. Four of them.”

“Yes, you are correct. We had four tickets. But at Villa San Giovanni, I placed them on the ground in front of my wife, to remind her what we would lose by waiting. For some reason, only God knows why, I never picked them up. They may still be there, but likely the wind took them, and they are in the bay, or someone found them and they will take our place on the boat. I am to blame for my carelessness, not Amaria. She followed her heart and her faith, and it is my fault.”

How can this be, to suffer for an act of charity? Where was the fairness?

“Non problema. You can buy more tickets,” I said. “This cannot ruin your family.”

He shook his head, sadly, as if he had seen the end of the world.

“No. Not possible, my friend. We are nearly broke...No. The truth of it is that we are broke. Any money to spare went to the priest…the goddamned priest. We have enough for maybe one ticket if I can sell her jacket in the next hour, and the clothes on our backs. No more than one ticket?”

Amaria and her daughter sobbed, but her son was quiet, swinging his feet in the wind.

“You cannot stay on the bench forever,” I said.

“Si. I know. We are desperate.” He stared at me, like a man dying of thirst, ashamed to ask for a drop of water. Between the three of us, maybe, we had money for one ticket, then it would leave us broke, and what would we say in America, when they ask to see our money; that we gave it to strangers because the mother was foolish, and the father was careless? They will kick our asses back to Sicilia. Then Emil did what I feared he would do. He asked for money, only a loan, not a gift, until America, and his brother would gladly pay us back.

What does a decent man do when his back against a wall? I lied.

“Emil, we have nothing. Not a lira between us, or we would gladly help you out. We are broke, God help us; not enough for even one ticket. We will be lucky if they let us into the country. I said those horrible words that have been with me these fifty years: I am sorry, but we cannot help.”

He said that we must put ourselves first, and he would do the same thing, but this is why the world is evil and uncaring, but he understood. “This life I wanted for my family, I left on the goddamned dock in Villa San Giovani.”

Amaria touched his shoulder, saying he should not curse in front of the children, no matter the reason, and he nodded, but never said he was sorry. I wish to God, he took it like a man, but he cried, he sobbed, and I was embarrassed for him.

“Why is papa crying,” the boy asked his mother. “Is papa sick?”

“Nothing to worry about,” she said, caressing his head and shoulders. “We have a problem, but your father will fix it, like always.” Emil was up and walking in a circle, and finally, almost settled and near calm, he took my hand.

We embraced, “arrivaderci, mio amico,” he said, a faint smile on his tired face. Amaria said good-bye, and waved a small, white handkerchief she held in her delicate hand. So feminine, I nearly wept myself. Then the truth hit me hard, and I was short of breath, that I would not see her again.

“Say good-bye to the others,” Emil said, “tell them we cannot go today. Maybe tomorrow. Say my brother was to meet us on the docks, and there is no way to contact him. If by chance you see him, Giuseppe D’arrico, tell him what happened and say hello from his brother and his cognata, Amaria Mastrianno and their children. Tell him we are alright…he should not worry, that we will be along shortly.”

“I will, if I see him. Arrivaderci, my friend” and I walked away and never looked back.

I freely admit that Emil asked for a loan. “We are broke,” I said, “Barely enough to survive.” He understood and apologized for being nothing more than a nuisance, and he was thankful we waited while his wife tended to the injured woman.

I said nothing to the others, not that they would care, except maybe Luca, who would need another woman to stare at and covet. Anyway, what would I say, that Emil is a stupid man to have lost their tickets, or he is weak and cannot control his woman? They would think him a fool, and he is not. I met the others near the gangplank, among the hordes of people heading to the ship, and I looked at the crowd and realized I knew them all, not a stranger did I see. They were with us on the road to Messina, in the ferry across the straits, in the cattle car to Napoli, and they were brothers and sisters, and we are all from the same family.

Enzo read the sign. “Third Class Cabins. Gangplank 1 and 2.”

Luca nudged me and pointed to a small wagon parked at the edge of the pier, the bastard who knocked Enzo down. Three workers unloaded baggage; in all twelve pieces were brought to the boat on what I saw as a private entrance. The woman supervising was tall and well-dressed, and as I recall she walked elegantly alongside her husband, who was dressed like an important man would dress. “These people,” I hear her say, “They look mean and hungry and dirty. Could they be diseased, maybe violent?” There were four in her party, including two children, and they boarded the boat on that private gangplank, away from the rabble. I was angry at what I saw; the privilege of wealthy men and women, thinking all, except them. are all violent and diseased and criminal.

I prayed to Santa Agata, that should I die on the boat, or in America, tell my family where I can be collected for burial. Do not leave me to rot, for the birds to eat, and if the boat is sinking, please do not think less of me because I do not help one of these bastards. I will not. No matter. I prayed for Grazia, the woman at the ferry, but I could not recall her last name. Is she still alive and was she worth it? Would she have done it for me, or Amaria and her family, or like the rest, would she have glanced at me on the ground and gone on her way, complaining how reckless I was to have broken a bone and why should she suffer for it?
