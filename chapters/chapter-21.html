Luca laughs. “A dead man’s poke, is that what you worry about.? You are a choir boy.”

He finds a passport, a pair of trousers and shirt, rolled together and tied with a piece of thin rope; a pocketknife (the blade rusted from the salt air, I believe) and a slim book, “The Life of Garibaldi.” A letter, but no money; not a single lira, yet no one admitted their disappointment. This poor girl could be anywhere in the country that is three thousand miles long, ocean to ocean, so I have been told.” I did not like that I knew her name, *Santina Benati, and the face of her father, Gennaro Benati, and her grandfather. Enzo was impatient with me. “Coincidence. Nothing more.”

Tomorrow, we dock, they say, but this is rumor, and most rumors are not true and never happen. If it is true, today is our last meal on this goddamned boat. Luca held his nose.

“Do we get off tomorrow?’ Santo asked Enzo, who never answered. This sailor was young and muscular, maybe thirty, an impressive looking man, not a hair out of place, or a whisker on his face. His uniform was freshly made up, boots polished to a high gloss, and he looked more like an officer than a sailor, except he carried a notebook, and we know men with notebooks are not to be trusted. I have said this before, I know, but I have a hatred for these people, that I come by honest. When I was a boy, about ten, in the year of the grande raccolto (great harvest) there was a shortage of men because so many moved north the year before. I was hired, with other children, to pick fruit by the bushel. The best harvest in years, but no more pay, not a miserable cent more. Take it or leave it. We were hungry. In front of him, to my surprise, was Assunta, from Colleberardi, dragging her bag along the ground. By the look of her face, all red and swollen, I believe she had been crying, or was about to cry. She asked Enzo, what should she do if her brother Enrico, and his friend, Bernardo, did not show, or they were lost, or decided not to come (she had not seen him since she was a child). His letter to the priest said the docking time of our ship would be printed in the Giornale (newspaper) and he would come on the streetcar with Bernardo. What would she do if left to her own? I had no answer, nothing to say, but I prayed she was wrong, and her brother will be waiting on the street, but for a feeling in my gut, I did not believe he would come. “Keep with us,” I said, and she nodded and wiped her eyes, and we both felt better. My idea was to ask a cop what she should do, where she should go, if her brother did not come. Ma and watch and wait, maybe pray. Her hands shook, like an old woman, or an infirm woman, and I could not recall a woman so defenseless and helpless, in my life.

“Stay close to me,” I said, and she did, though I did not offer to carry her bag, but for some reason but I felt responsible for this girl I barely knew. This, maybe, was my flaw. Enzo said there were millions of people in this city, no chance we would find him if he did not want to be found. Where did he live? How would she get back home?

The wind changed, now from the south; we smelled horse, and for what reason I thought of *Signore Gazarello’s two great draught mares, that ate, and shit more than they worked, but he loved them both, swearing they were like a part of his family. This I could not understand. Two animals are family? Two horses? A pathetic man, but I liked him and respected him, as he was a good friend to my father. Near hidden by the shadows, two cops sat high and proud on browns, watching the line of men and women struggle with their children, dragging all they owned in a bag, maybe two. Watching us, Luca said. How are we men who deserve to be watched, like criminals? We are nothing but nervous and frightened, and women with children, who worry they will never see their family again. Then I hear yelling and uproar and my heart began to beat like a loud drum. The line stopped, sudden, and a thousand tired feet were frozen to the cobblestones (there were fifty, or so, in our line).

I am curious, on the why of things, but not interested in another man’s explanation, so I step out of line to see for myself, for a better look and I saw him, a big man near the gate, who was tall, a head taller than me, angry face and vicious eyes, sunk deep in his skull. He rubbed the handle of this thick club he carried at his side, taking some pleasure in the feel of it. The boat was behind him, through the gate he guarded, with twenty men who blocked our way. Maybe more. I could not tell, or I was afraid to take another look. He was their leader and without warning he charged at our line, swinging this club around his head, beating the cold air, and cursing like a crazy man. He stopped his frenzy, and pointed the club to the cold, blue water; the wind changed direction and calmed, like some sign, that he would be heard. He was broad and muscular and stood, his feet wide apart, threatening, and I admit the look of it made me nervous. He took some pleasure in the physical things, like screaming to threaten other men, even women. I admit he looked tougher than Luca looked. Like Conte, he was a commanding man, but his knees were not broken in pieces like Conte’s, and he was younger and not well dressed, but he could scream to wake the dead.

“Go back, all you bastards…where you come from and die. You are not like us, and not welcomed here.” The men behind him cheered, as they closed ranks. It was what they said, and the back slapping that bothered me . Wops and dago’s. all of us; that’s what they said, even to the women and children. The words needed no translating; I heard them on the boat from the german crew, and from other men, not Italiano. Their shrill voices, the insults, echoed from the buildings, mixing with the wind. This anger, I have not forgotten, and I hear it every day, from that day. A young woman, a mother, afraid of what she saw. pulled a child tight to her side.

“Che sta dicendo. Will they kill us?” she pleads of Enzo, who shook his head. “What have we done to them?”

“They just do not want us here,” he answered. She grabbed his sleeve, but he pulled away. I saw terror in her face, and she cried, her child cried.

“Where will we go if they make us leave?” Enzo never answered but shook his head. She took the boy and disappeared in the crowd.

The big man, with the big mouth, came close to our line, dragging his club across the cobblestones, laughing how the devil must laugh. He inspected each man, each woman, as he passed where were we stood, and when he stopped, he stopped in front of me, and I see in the squint of his eye, the love of battle. I thought, thank God, he is not a heathen, as I see the sun reflect off the gold crucifix he wore around his neck. I relaxed at the sight of the symbol of Christ he wore, but with no warning he ran his club into my ribs, like a bayonet. The people behind me screamed. Assunta from Colleberardi, cried out, “Lascialo solo,” leave him alone.

“Why me,” I asked as I fell to the ground, and taking down the old man who carried the house on his back. My grandfather’s vines fell from the case, scattering in all directions. Keep the vines in sight, I was thinking when my head hit stones. I moaned, I was groggy, but not hurt. Sicilian heads are hard, thank God, like the cobblestone. Santo gives me a hand up, I slip and went down again. Luca screamed, like a crazy man, that we should fight, and he moves toward this sonofabitch, ready to confront him alone. Assunta looks down at me, where I lay on the cobblestone. “Sei ferito? (are you hurt), ” she asks, frightened and out breath. I tell her stand back, away from the cavones, and she did. I clenched my firsts so hard the fingers turned white and lost all feeling. I looked for the cops on their horses and I knew this crazy bastard was not done with me. When he cocked his club for another run, I thought better of fighting, of giving him a head to beat, certain he would and enjoy himself. I back away, out of club range, and put out my hand to start the peace; maybe foolishly. He laughed; he was defiant. Luca curses the big man then lunged, but I pulled him back to the line, holding on as hard as I could, while he screamed, that he wanted to fight. “No more Conte’s,” he yells, and we understood what he meant. Enzo settled him down and they moved away from the front, while this crazy bastard, laughed and taunted, as the men behind him screamed, and the cops looked away, and talked among themselves.

Si, it is, but tomorrow it is supposed to rain.

As to this crazy sonofabitch, he screamed and carried on. “You are parasites (parassiti),” as he struck his club on the ground. When he tired, he stopped and pointed it at our line.

“Siete tutti parassiti,” he said (his Italiano was pretty good). “You are parasites.”

When he finished his rant, this man with a crucifix, he runs back to his line and mixed in with the others. Five horse cops finally move in, between us and them. I believed this was the end of the violence, but no, the cops were not tough, and these bastards cursed (there were women and children in the line). The cops were impatient, saying they wanted to leave, and when they pulled back six of these sonofabitches moved towards us, still led by the man who proudly wore his crucifix around his neck. We stayed our ground, mostly, but the older men and the women and their children moved back, fearing the violence. Who would protect us if we went forward? The American cops? What fool, except a rich fools would trust the cops. Not us. One of the six men, short and fat, raised a fist to a straggler, a woman he had cornered, but a horse cop came and chased him away, but the horse kicked up, knocking the women down (I do not believe on purpose). She lay still on the cobblestones, crying and holding her head, but I believe she was not hurt all that much, as there was no blood to be seen. Her husband gave chase to this bastard who cornered his wife, but the horse cop cut him off, and we see he laughed at getting away. Her bag split open, and what she carried scattered across the cobblestone. We pick her up and gathered her goods, but she limped when she walked. Her husband spoke some English, but poorly, as Enzo could tell. She pleaded to her man, “take me home,” but he held her hand and stoked her hair, saying they had no home.

She stared with her sorrowful eyes. “I remember,” she said, and began to weep. “We have no home.”

A cop, a boss, as I recall he wore gold, told the men at the gate they could not block the way, and he ordered us not to incite them, since this was America and if they did not want us here, they had the right to say so. “Uncivilized,” I thought. The men obeyed, but slowly, taking their time, eyeing each of us before they left, a few mouthing off to the cops that they were traitors to their race. More But more came from nowhere and took their place. These new men, were younger and bigger and louder, and carried shovels and sledgehammers, striking them on the ground in cadence, like a marching army. These protestors were assoles, all of them; they wore the same clothes, work clothes, like me, and their hands and faces were dirty. One man threw his shovel to the side and unfolded an American flag, and the men behind him cheered and carried on. The flag snapped in the wind, and I admit it was a sight, like the green woman; they chanted, called us “wops,” said we should go home, and not dirty their clean country, and cages were too good for us.

One man carried a sign I believe said the same in bold and red letters.

“Wops go home.” He held it high over his head, waving it like a flag.

“Wops go home,” he screams, again, looking me straight in the eye.

I made this promise, to me and no one else, that I would never leave this country until I was ready to leave, or they put a gun to my head, and I had no choice. Men like these men, were the men who drove me from my home, and they will not do it again.

The ferry was setting to leave, and I would be on it, walking or feet first. I start for the gate; my legs shaking, and I near tripped when I caught the heel of my boot on a high stone. At the final gate he met me, this man with the sign, and the cop yelled to him, that he could not block my way. The cavone laughed. Do not embarrass him, I thought; leave him an honorable way out, so he can feel like a man, even if he is not much of a man. I prayed for a peaceful end, as I extended my hand, once more. Slowly, and stuttering, more like a fool, I said, “mi chiamo…my name…my name…Antonino Sparacino…we cannot go back…there is no other place to go.” When I stared at him, what did I see right off? Like the crazy bastard with the club, this man had a crucifix around his neck, silver, not gold, and I figured he was not a boss. Bosses wear gold, always. What I saw was a hateful man, but still a man. What did he see? Did he see a man? Not a man, an animal, maybe, not good enough to cage. He refused to speak, or did I see any emotion on his weathered face, except he looked at the cop on the nervous horse, that galloped in place. When I give him my hand, like Americani’s do, he laughed and spits at me, a good one, a hit across my right cheek. I clenched my fists, and my first thought was to punch his face, but what would be the good of it? I wiped his spit off my cheek, and said it again, “mi chiamo Antonino Sparacino…happy I am here.” Someday, maybe, we will meet again and sit on a chair in the park and share a bottle of wine, and maybe he will say why I am his enemy, and maybe I will understand, but probably I will not. I gave him my hand once more, and he refused.

“I am a proud American,” he yelled into my face. He turned and looked at the men with him. “Born here like my father and his father, and all these men,” and they applauded, and he smiled and struck his chest in a frenzy.

The wind kicked up.

“Do you hear what I said? I am American,” and they applaud him again.

He pushed the heavy sign into my face, and when I push back, he was startled and fell off balance. I see the horse cop moving close and of course he saw it but chose to turn away. I could hear myself breathe, the rattling in my chest. This guardian of the gate was making a choice. It seemed a long time when nothing happened, then he spit at my feet, and when he looked up, he looked at Assunta, from Colleberardi, and said of her, in decent Sicilianu, “ puttanu,” whore,” and without thinking I swung the poke filled with my grandfather’s grapes, hitting this bastard square up the side of his head. He fell, hard, and hit the cobblestone hard, and he lay there rubbing his face and cursing me to hell. I stood over him, fists clenched, but to this day, I cannot recall what I said, only that I was angry and babbling. Foolishness, maybe, but I do not think so. Luca was there, next to me, and he grabbed my arm. Luca the peacemaker. Two men picked this rabble off the ground, and the three stood side by side. He took his sign, “Wops Do Not Belong Here,” and broke off the board and stood defiantly with the heavy stick in his hand, that I believe he thought to club me. Luca grabbed my arms, overpowering me with his great strength, and as I struggle, he sends the girl from Colleberardi to the back of the line. She was scared and crying, but she did what she was told, like a good Italian woman. Two rowdies grabbed the bag and spread her clothes over the cobblestone. If this was to be war, then so be it. But the big mouth at the gate was no fool, as I first thought. A few men from our line moved to the front, and the young ones clenched their firsts, but we did not curse, or yell, or threaten. In the end, this crazy bastard did not step aside, but he moved enough that we could fit our way between him and the fence, and toward the ferry. It was a win, of a sort, but he never apologized for insulting this young girl, when he called her puttanu, whore, but I left him a message written to the side of his face; a thick, red welt to remember me (my name on it, Antonino Sparacino) and the rule that a civilized man does not call a young girl “puttanu.” I stopped and starred at this sonofabitch, but Enzo took my arm, telling me in Sicilianu, that I bested this rabble, and it was time to “get to it.”

I pat at my pockets, took hold of my suitcase, my poke, and stared at the gate. I was like the old man who carried his house on his back; everything I owned was in my pockets or my suitcase or the poke I carried. What did I have? My grandfather’s vines. A worthless pair of worn pants; my father’s shirt; government papers; a few lire to hold me until I found work. There were other things, and I am ashamed to say it, because some will think less of me, but now is too late for me to worry on it. Wrapped in brown paper and hidden at the bottom of my suitcase, the fine pair of leather gloves, and the locket I took from the old capu’s poke when I was alone. I took the picture from the locket, leaving it in the capu’s suitcase. Carmela still wears the locket and to my shame I have lied to her all these years, swearing I found it on the docks in Napoli. She knows, but she does not know. I refused to leave it for the sailors to fight over, when they found the capu’s suitcase, they would sell it all to buy wine and whores. How could it be right to leave these fine things behind? The gloves? The locket? Later, much later, I confessed to Patri Antonelli (a Sicilian from Palermu) at Our Lady of Pompeii Church, who understood the bad times, but he said it was wrong what I did, and gave me penance; three Hail Mary’s, two Our Fathers and a good Act of Contrition. When I left the church that day, it was late in the afternoon, and I run into a good friend and a good baker, Sig. Leonardo di Piesta, from Itala, in the Sicilian hills. He come across on the same boat, although we did not meet, but this made us brothers.
