Santo stared across the horizon, at the lights of the city. “They are beautiful when they sparkle, like stars.” Enzo cleared his throat.

“Well, my friend. It looks like they are sparkling.” He smiled. “But it is the dust in the air playing tricks on the eyes. Nothing more. The light does not actually sparkle, or twinkle as some say.” Forever after that day, I wished he said nothing; never again did I enjoy the beauty of what I imagined to be magical. I changed the subject before he destroyed more of what were my dreams. More important to me than lights, was this man, himself. Why is he here, with us? He is educated. A teacher (if he was). I wanted to ask him straight out. Was he running from the cops? Maybe a woman? Another man’s wife? Did he owe to the Black Hand? And how much?

My balls. Where were they? Do I feel for them, in front of five hundred?

I will ask another time.

A husky sailor, with a thick, black beard, pushed his way to the railing. “Move aside.” We separated and let him pass. He loosened the ropes holding the ship and dropped them to a sailor on the dock. Enzo patted my shoulder and smiled, and I smiled back, like old friends, but were not. “There is no turning back,” Enzo said. “What we find will be our future. What we leave is our past, and dead.”

I felt a chill. I should have asked. Mama and papa? Were they my past?

The engines struggled hard, the deck vibrated, and the boat felt as if it was falling apart. A frightened young woman grabbed at the rail. “Are we sinking?” she asks Enzo.

“No, signora, the engines are turning over…perfectly normal,” and she believed him and thanked him, but her hands turned white from her tight grip on the rail.

Somewhere near the middle of the ship, I hear this beautiful music, that was pure and clean, as it floated through the heavy night air. Neither the rattle of the engines, or the sounds of the sea, could kill it, or did the people, or the wind.

I ask Enzo, “the music…who is singing?”

“A priest,” he said, without emotion. “And a Scalabrinan, I believe.”

“Scalabrinan? I have not heard of them. Are they Catholic?”

“Yes, of course. They help Italiani immigrants in America. You and me, my friend.”

I abandon my good spot at the railing and wandered toward the crowd, looking for this man who sang beautiful music, as just hearing him was not enough. I wanted to see his face and hold his hand and ask for his blessing. Enzo followed, but not Santo, who has said music is of no interest to him.

“He sings in Latin,” Enzo said, pointing to the priest. “German music. Franz Shubert’s Ave Maria. He sings like an inspired man…as beautiful as I have heard it sung…better than any German could sing it.” There were tears in Enzo’s eyes, and I turned away, pretending not to see. But I cried, too, and felt no shame.

So, it is called the Ave Maria. I have heard it before, in the Chiesa di Sant’Agatha. I believe it was sung by Giuseppina Spucci, the wife of Andrea Battaglia; she sang it well, but not powerful, like this priest, and she could not make me cry, like a woman. A crowd pressed close around him, listening; no one moved or made a sound, not even the children who obeyed and did not stir. We were all sad at leaving home, except the children who did not seem to understand, but they cried if their family cried, and all the women cried, but only a few of the men, and me. I turned away that no one could see, though I would not have been ashamed if they did.

“He praises the Blessed Virgin,” Enzo said. “He asks her blessing for all of us, even heathens, if there are any on the boat. Listen to the music, my friend. You will feel better...you will lose your fear of what is coming.” For the moment, at least, his music calmed my fear and soothed my sadness at leaving home. It would not be honest to say I felt good about it, but soothed.

He was a tall priest and a fine-looking young man; he stood alone, his black robe cracking in the wind, and he held a book in both hands. I am sure the women in his parish were sad he has been sent to America, that he would no longer watch over their souls. His voice had great power and was overwhelming, and most wept while he sang, and he sung until the ship was free of the currents and away. In the end, when he had finished, I swear I could hear his music echoing across the winds. He walked through the crowd of the faithful, and they parted, and at that moment we were all faithful, and he gave his blessing to all. I knelt in front of him, asking that he pray for my family who I left in Sicilia on their own. He would remember them in his prayers, he said, kissing a small cloth he held tightly in his hand, then touched it to my forehead and I felt a shiver in my arms.

In nomine Patris et fillii et Spiritus Sancti.
