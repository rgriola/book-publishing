The Inheritance of Memory

I have no good memories of Calabria. I was forced to be here, against my will when I was drafted to soldier in the Kings Army. Who asked me if I wanted to be here? No one asked. The people in Calabria, and I swear an oath to this, are rude and loud by nature, and hold the Siciliani in low regard, though it is well known we are the superior people.

They say, these Calabrese, “everything is better in Calabria.” The food, the wine, all better, and the women more beautiful than anywhere in Italia. And Virtuoso(virtuous). I know better. The puttanu who followed the army are Calabrese women, who did not hear that all Calabrese women are virtuoso. The food is foul, with no flavor, like wet paper, and their wine is harsh and sour and whole barrels spoil overnight. Signuri *Cosimo Rinaldi, a Siciliani who married a Calabrese woman of some means, owned a farm near the camp where I trained, when not guarding piles of wood and wagons with no wheels, I worked for him and what he paid I kept, sending my army pay home; it helped, papa said, although a few times it was lost, or stolen in the mail; mostly stolen. Never give mail to an officer, as he is likely to lose it, or steal it.

When my time in Calabria was near over, Rinaldi came to see me in the field. “Stay,” he said. “Marry my daughter, *Giuseppina is in need of a husband.” Why me? He respected me. I knew how to sweat, he said, and any man who ‘sweated’ like I ‘sweated’ would always support a wife and children, and relatives (him and his wife) in their old age. “You honor me,” I said, “but…” and at ‘but’ he took my arm, and we walked the farm he promised would be mine when he passed. I was so afraid his offer would trap me there, I stuttered like a fool. “Your daughter, Giuseppina, she is too women for me,” I said, meaning she was twice too much, not slim, like a woman should be. He believed I had complimented his daughter, that she was someone too good for me; he thanked me, saying he understood, but should I change my mind, his offer was open unless he found a better man for his daughter. I stopped to see him the day I left, to thank him for the work and hospitality. He gave me tomatoes, and other vegetables for my trip, and wine for my father, in a green bottle made in Napoli. When papa took his first taste, he laughed and poured the rest in a ditch, saying, “Like piss from a Calabrese cesspool, but I like the green bottle,” and he kept it on the windowsill, and bragged to his friends how it came from Napoli.
