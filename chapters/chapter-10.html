Chapter 10

Fellino's Grappa and the Bitter Dreams

The Smell of the Sea and the Call for Help

I see off in the night, flickering lights, like beacons at sea. We were getting close to the city. I thanked God and Santo got on his knees and thanked God and made the sign of the cross. It was near nine o’clock at night, by Santo’s old and tarnished watch, a gift from his grandfather, but it kept good time. At the edge of the city, we see an old man on the ground, but not asleep, more leaning against a splintered hitching post, his covering his eyes, as if to shut out the world.

“Buonasera,” I said, but he only grunted and otherwise ignored me.

“The ferry, signuri? Can you give directions to the ferry?” I asked him politely, but instead he snorted and coughed and looked away. Santo checked the old man up and down. “Is he deaf?”

Luca pushed Santo aside. “The ferry,” he yelled, like a mad overseer, and the old man was startled and leaned right, and pointing down the deserted street.

“Un miglio,” he said. ‘A mile, or so…a short ways…and I meant no disrespect, but I am weak and hungry…do you have food…maybe grappa?”

“No. Nothing to spare,” Luca said, annoyed and pushing at my back to move me along.

Santo stared down at the man, who had slid from the post and lay flat on the ground. “I will pray for you,” he said, making the sign of the cross. The old man shook his head. “Prayers? You see me half dead and you offer prayers? I would rather have a plate of beans, maybe a turnip, if you can spare one. I have not eaten in two days.”

We walked on and did not look back, but to this day I hear his plea for help.

In two or three blocks the street began to fill in with people. The buildings were old, but the cobblestone was pretty new, and we see black, iron hitching posts planted on each side of the street. Not a horse to be seen. How was it a great city survive without no horses or mules? We have been here before, at least me and Luca (Santo has never been anywhere). But I knew little of the city, as I was here just a few months, and never walked the streets searching for cheap whores, like the rest of the men, who said this part of Messina was best for finding puttanu. Thank God, I never shared the vile weakness of these men, as they were likely to suffer sores and other maladies, and some would die in agony(Patri Carmine said this). God rest their souls, these men, but I have never prayed for them.

Santo nudged my arm, his eyes bugging. “My God. These people. Where do they all come from?”

Luca laughed. “People? This is nothing. There will be ten times the number in the morning. They live underground.” He smiled. “Some crawl out from the cracks and dance all night, and in the morning, when the sun comes up, the rest join them.” Santo stared at the cracks as we walked, complaining on a pain in his neck that kept his head low for relief. At the next streetlamp we come on this carriage, fancy painted, and drawn by a good a good- and healthy- looking mare. She was nervous and wanted to go and pranced in place. A well-attired man stood next to the carriage and looked to be a class never of man never seen in Sicilia. When we came within a few feet, he put his hands in the air and waved us down.

“Scusa,” he said, molto politely. “I am looking for Via Umberto. Do you know where it is?” A good month ago, he said, the wife of his oldest son gave birth to a child. “A boy, thank God.” But they just received the letter, and were anxious to see the child, especially his wife, who is happy to finally be grandmother to a child, and it was a boy. A girl would have been acceptable, but a boy was better. “We are from Caserta, near Napoli and have not seen our son for a year. My wife says it is her duty to help with the child, but we cannot find his street and it is getting late.” He looked up and down the street. “And this part of town…my wife is nervous. No offense to you men, of course.”

“No apology needed, signuri,” I said. “But we are strangers here, and probably lost.” He nodded that he understood, but was grateful we stopped, and thanked us for our time. He handed me a coin, that I refused.

Luca gives me a dirty look. “Grosso colpo,” he said. “Big shot.”

Grappa, Whores, and Low Life

After a few long blocks, the street narrowed in, barely wide enough for a double cart and a pair of well-built draught horses. And deserted, again. Luca’s mole people went back through the cracks, leaving us near alone on the street, but for a few soldiers and sailors, mostly leaning against the buildings, or staggering on the street. Luca pushed his elbow into my ribs.

“Remember the days, Antonino?” He laughed. “Good grappa…cheap whores.” “Grappa and whores? No, Luca, I do not recall those days.”

We see four men at a table in front of a shabby bar, and by their looks I figured them to be seadogs off one of the iron freighters, or some other derelict boat in the harbor. They were noisy and rowdy, but they were not Navy, though I have seen sailors who were noisy and rowdy and drunk at shabby bars. These four? I believe they had been bested by grappa, as each man had a near empty bottle within his reach. They were playing cards, Briscola, I think, and they ignored us, thank God, as these men go violent if bothered. Luca said he was dead tired but needed a kick in the ass that only grappa could do. Me? My legs were stiff and tired from the long walk; I wanted only to rest, grappa being of no interest to me.

It is a poor plan we made. Grappa, but no place to spend the night. Santo wet his lips. “We should have a last drink in Sicilia. Who knows for how long?”

“Maybe there is grappa in America.” Santo said. Luca stared him down.

“Maybe they have grappa in America? If they is no grappa in America, what is the sense of going?”

“We stop for one drink,” I said. “Who has money to piss away on two?”

Luca slapped my shoulder. “Big shot. You should have taken the coin, my friend.”

Santo repeated he did not care to stop, and sitting in a bar, as every civilized man knows, is a waste of good life, and the men at the table looked to be of the low life and disorderly kind. His real fear, I knew, was Luca, because grappa changes him, even one drink, or the thought of the drink. He is not falling down, or does he piss himself (not often) but when he drinks, there is no reasoning with him. Two, maybe three drinks at most and he is no longer Luca, but some man I do not trust. Santo drinks grappa, but he says no, never, not once, but I have shared grappa with him. He claims to dislike the harshness of the sting, but the truth is he does not like to pay, so preaching on the evil of drink suits his purpose (except his uncles’ wine, that he drinks free).

Luca raises his right hand.

“One drink and no more,” he promised, but Santo knew the truth, and complained on the look of the bar and the shabby men at the table, so we walk on looking for a place suitable to his gentle nature. But as we walked, the meaner, more violent the men became and the buildings, more rundown, and the street older, and the cobblestones loose and heaving, not tight to the ground. We were closer to the waterfront than before, as I see civilization was falling part. Who cares about the waterfront, with its derelicts and fallen women, and contadini who abandon Sicilia for a better place, like we are doing?

We loitered where two streets crossed, watching the men go by, and the carriages and wagons bounce across the rough cobblestone, and the oil lamps flicker and smoke. Luca recalled a bar further on, run by some character he had met, but could not recall his name. The bar was clean, and the drinks were cheap. He swore it to be true.

The Bar of the Oldest Brother

We come to this bar that Luca remembered from the ‘old days,’ a battered stone building, lifeless, but he acted like it was home. “It has not changed a bit,” he said, rubbing his hand across a sign with faded red letters and a thick wooden door, that has not seen paint in years. A harmless looking old man sat alone at a round table, asleep; his head thrown back and propped by the high back chair. “That’s him,” Luca said. “I remember, now. Fellino is his name.” On second look the old man could have been dead, as I could not see him breathe when I stared on his chest. I thought to leave right off, as I saw only round tables and I never feel right about leaning on a round table. They were freshly painted, each surrounded by four new chairs, it seemed. The paint job was inferior, though, with runs and drips and bubbles in the finish, and the floor was covered with splatters of paint. The air smelt of linseed oil, wine and grappa, mixed together, but it was a pleasant smell.

The bar was nothing, a bad sin, so I saw no reason for Luca to remember it, but he swore on being drunk there, at least once, probably many more times than one. I saw a disgraceful place, far worse than the bar in the piazza back home, but Luca demanded we stay for one drink, maybe two, as it was promised to him. He picked the table and sat first, his back to the wall and facing the door. He felt safe, as he could see anyone coming for him, a part of his fantasy. I took a chair and right off I see the dead bodies; six fleas, or some other bug, or parts of bugs, flattened and buried in the paint and staring at me from their grey grave. Luca went to wake the owner, but he tripped on the proud edge of a floorboard and fell against the old man’s chair, who was startled, and he stood up and smoothed his rumpled shirt, apologizing, and swore he was only resting his eyes, trying to throw off a terrible headache; a malady he suffered his whole life.

“I own this fine place,” he boasted, then complaining he is forced to be the waiter, as his regular man left him for America, or Argentina; he was not sure, and in truth he did not care, and his worker left a poor man and would return a poor man, but he wanted to kill the waiter because he stole money and food for his family.

“Good riddance to the sonofabitch,” he said, his hands trembling. “I cannot believe I treated this cavone like a son, when customers told me I was a fool. They knew him, I did not. Steal from me…food, money…the sonofabitch.” He kicked the closest chair, knocking it half way across the room, cursing the bastard waiter to hell. “I am glad he is gone…trouble, nothing but trouble.” He raged about this ingrate and what he did for him; that he put shoes on his feet and clothes on his back. Then he spots an entombed flea on the tabletop, and runs his hand across it, denying it was a bug, but a run and he would take care of it when he could, as the paint was still soft, and it could be peeled back with a knife and smoothed with rough paper.

“Maybe another table?” he asked. “If you came for a meal, you will be disappointed.”

An impatient man, as I could see, always tapping his fingers on something, so I figure he was well-to-do, as they are generally short with men of our kind. He warned again, he had only drink to sell, wine and grappa, some whiskey, but nothing to eat. No food at all. “I know an inn with no food is a foolish thing, but it is all gone…the food.” He shrugged his shoulders in a way that said he did not give a shit what we thought. “I kept a few loaves for myself, but they are not for sale.” Luca laughed.

“Not for sale?” he said, aping the bar keeps words. “Then you are the one saloon owner who would not sell his mother’s teeth for a little cash.” Fellino was not insulted.

“You are correct. The bread I kept is not for sale, at any price. But if by some chance I do sell it to you, remember this, it is stale and will not be to your liking.” He shook his finger, “And there will be no discount. You pay full price. Take it or leave it.” He named the drinks he offered for sale; Sicilian wine, some grappa and a bottle of French liquor, he would not recommend, as the French were like rats, and their liquor tasted like old piss. This is what he said, and he said it proudly and I do believe he hated the French. Fellino was in his sixties, maybe seventies, as it was hard to tell since he was not a well-kept man, but his clothes were of suitable wear for a bar owner, and his hair was cut, and he seemed clean and did not smell, but there was this look in his eyes, that maybe he was tired of living.

The Price of Leaving and the Spit in the Glass

Luca leaned in close to me, too close; I did not enjoy being so close to another man. “Fellino knew how to show men a good time. Good food and good drink and plenty of women always just a few lire away. I believe he had two or three in his own stable. Good stamina, for an old man, they said about him.”

Fellino, I could see, was an emotional man, drunk or sober, and it was not the wine that brought it on, but memories, they say. Mostly he cried about the old days and how they all left him, promising to return, but did not. He was tough in his day, and well-connected in Messina, and above all a man who knew how to give favors and receive favors in return; well known to the cops and the politicians, who drank and ate and played “gratuito.” Luca put his hand on the old man’s shoulder.

“You remember me, Fellino, I have been here before.” Fellino nodded but did not answer. Luca put out his hand to renew an old acquaintance, but Fellino only shook his head. “I do not remember you. Where you a soldier? You all looked alike to me.”

“Si. Three years ago. The army. I spent many nights here.”

“I am sorry, you are not familiar.” He moved close and stared into Luca’s face, and squinted, finally shaking his head. “I do not know you, signuri. I am sorry.”

“I drank here every night…Luca Ponto…the army…the whores?”

“Mi dispiace. As I said, you all look the same to me.” It was embarrassing for Luca that he was not remembered that he was nothing special and did not stand out in Fellino’s mind, and he bitched to Fellino that he spent most of his pay across the bar, and to be forgotten after a few years was an insult.

Fellino shrugged. “Mi dispiace,” he said again. Luca pointed to the back room. “Antonella. Call her out here.” The old man stares at Luca.

“You know Antonella?”

“I do. A beautiful girl. Is she here? Send her out.”

Fellino turns and spits on the floor. “Se n'è andata,” he said, sorrowfully, “She is gone. She does not work here anymore. I fired the puttanu when she took up with that cavone, Umberto Rastelli…a no good…like his father, May they all rot in hell.” Santo made the sign of the cross.

“Hell is bad,” Luca said. “No disrespect to you, or did I mean to call up bad memories, but she was a fine…” Fellino raised his voice.

“Do you men want bread, or not?” he said, looking straight at Luca. “If you stay, I give no discounts…full price.”

Luca waved Fellino away. “Forget the god damned bread. Bring us three grappa’s, not the pigswill, that piss water you sell the sailors and bring good glasses. Clean, not those iron cups all rusted on the bottom. I still taste the iron in my mouth.” This charge of bad service upset the old man that he could barely speak. He looked square at me, for some reason, not Luca, his real tormentor, and slapped, hard, on the tabletop. “Your friend. He has a big mouth. He should sit with his back to the wall.”

I apologized for Luca, but the old man was angry and turned his back to me.

“This pigswill, as he calls it, I do not serve it to paying customers, only the cops and other free loaders, but after I spit in the glass.” He leaned and stared at Luca. “The paying customers get my best and I only serve the best. It has always been my rule.” He stiffens his chest, like a proud man and snapped his wide, black suspenders, that the undertakers wear.

“Did you hear me? What I said? I spit in the glass for the freeloaders. Something a little extra on the house. As you see, free is not free in Fellino’s place.” I laughed, it was funny, and Luca laughed, but not Santo, who said he did not see the humor.

“Men can get sick,” he said. Luca pulled Santo from his chair and hugged him around the neck, until he managed to pull away.

“Were you born yesterday?” he says to Santo. “He spits in the glass to make these bastards sick…his revenge…this is how he fights. The grappa is free, but it is not free. Capisce?” “I know why he does it. But this is dangerous and against the law of God.”

“It is about suffering, Luca. This man makes the suffering worse, not better…like the woman and the boy a few miles back, and this famine. He spits in the glass of people he does not like…he makes it all the worse. Patri Carmine would say it, too.”

Fellino was angry. Not furious or violent, but he wanted to know what this talk was all about. “Famine? These people you speak of…this priest, and a woman…a boy? Who are they?”

Santo was confused. “Patri Carmine is our priest back home, and a great man, who has worked with us in the fields and sweated like we sweated. These people we speak of, a woman and a boy, they live near here and are starving. I admit I was not concerned, but it bothers me now. We hear there is famine here. Is it true?”

Fellino gave a heavy sigh. “Well,” he looked at us and scratched his right arm and complained about the heat. “You ask an interesting question, I admit.” He scratched his face as he thought. “Well, uh, yes,” and he paused for the right words. “We do have a famine, but a small one, only a small one, and of no consequence as famines go, except there has been a long drought. And, yes, regrettably, a few have suffered, mostly the foolish ones. And a few have died. People die every day from many things.” He smiled. “But I have not, so how bad is it?” and realizing he never introduced himself, he stood straight and stiff(but not tall, as he was a short man). “I am Carmine Fellino, proud son of Giuseppe Fellino and Anna Porti, my sainted mother.” He was not of this city, he said, but born near Spadafora, north of Messina and close to the sea, where in his youth he fell asleep each night listening to its pleasant sound. His father, long since dead, opened this bar years ago, before Garibaldi conquered Sicilia (ruining it forever, he said). Lo stupor di Sicilia, the ‘rape of Sicilia,’ his father called it, cursing Garibaldi, and finally dying a broken man because of him. Fellino was tired and almost finished, worn by the day, he said. Could he sit and share our table? He brought grappa and three glasses, setting one at each place, and the bottle in the middle of the table.

“What is the smell,” Santo asked Fellino?

“Paint and grappa,” he replied, apologizing for this calamity, promising, his right hand to God, the smell would not make us sick, as it has lingered three days and he was healthy as a horse. To blame for this was a deadbeat customer he allowed (against his better judgment)to work off a grappa debt, by painting the tables and chairs. The quality of the work was poor, but to be expected, and his only hope was to recover his money, but the deadbeat was a drunk and a roustabout, not to be trusted, like the rest of his family, who refused to make the debt good.

“A mistake I will never make again.”

He asked, again, could he join us; his legs were on fire from standing all day, and he cursed the “goddamnable heat,” as he wiped his forehead with a wet bar rag.

“Si, of course.” Luca pulled a chair from the table.

Fellino smiled and bowed from the waist, like a gentleman would do.

“Grazie.” He took a tall glass from his pocket and set to the edge of the table. His hand shook as he poured, and the grappa spilt, some on the table and some on the floor, but he did not seem to care, saying his only desire was to see us raise our glass to a better Sicilia, blessed by God.

“Salutè…salutè a tutti.”

The Traitors and the Coward

We raised our glasses proudly and there was the sweet sound of that fine ring when good the glasses are touched. “Salutè,” Luca repeated as he put the glass to his lips, smiled and threw straight back, swallowing in a single noisy gulp; he held his breath, while his hands and shoulders shook.

“Good grappa,” he said. “Smooth, like sweet water, but in the end like the kick of a mule, not to be forgotten or ignored…the best I have ever had. And now a toast to America,” and Fellino’s ears perk, like a cat who spots a mouse.

“I thought as much. You goddamned men are going to America. ”

Luca nodded. “Si, America. Tomorrow, we take the ferry to Calabria…if it is running.” Fellino chest heaved as he sighs a heavy sigh, and I see how black his eyes were, how deep they set into his head. He was no happy man, for all he had.

“Every sonofabitch is going to America these days...even my goddamned waiter.” He starred over Luca’s shoulder at the dull, gray wall behind him. “Sooner than later, this godforsaken island will be empty. Who will be left, but old men, like me, and horses and mules, and no one young enough or foolish enough to waste their lives planting and mucking stalls?” Did I ever muck a stall, he asked me? I did, I said.

“Did it make a man out of you?” he asked.

“It did nothing for me, as I can tell, except maybe it ruined my back.” He agreed. “I mucked plenty, and it did nothing for me.”

He relaxed and wiped spilt grappa from the table with the palm of his hand. “You men are chasing a shooting star, and who has ever caught a shooting star?” He slapped the tabletop hard, and it made a hollow sound and he stared at his hand, like a baby would do, then wiped it across his pant leg.

“My brothers, both of them, went off to America. Years ago. They hated farming and farmers, like death, and the sulphur mines. My God, how they hated the sulfur mines. Like our sainted mother they wanted a better life. Better life? What the hell is a ‘better life,’ I asked them. If you suffer less than the man next to you, then you have the better life. Does this take a great thinker to understand? They were young, then. The day they left, great promises flowed from their mouths. We will write, and you will join us,’ they said and like a fool, I believed they were sincere.” He wiped his eyes with the bar rag slung over his shoulder, apologizing for his show of weakness, that is never manly. “How many years have they been gone? I cannot recall. Ten, maybe twenty. Probably thirty.” He rested his folded hands on the table, like men, but mostly women, do when they pray, and he sighed again. I was embarrassed watching him fall apart.

Men should not sigh out of frustration, or carry on, or weep for any reason. Even great pain. He turned to the wall and cleared his throat.

“I have two brothers. Carmelo Fellino and Giovanni Fellino. Carmelo is my youngest brother, the most reckless, and I know I will never see him again, on this side of the river, as he was shot down in the street. Butchered, like an animal. Now he is dead and not reckless anymore.” What is the right to say to a stranger, whose brother was murdered years past, who I never knew? I do not morn dead men to much, except family and only if they have treated me right, and I have never mourned a stranger. Santo pushed his grappa glass to the center of the table and settled back in his chair.

“Did he fight with cops? Was it the Carabinieri?”

Fellino cursed and shook his finger in Santo’s face. “Not a cop,” he said. “Does this surprise you? If you knew my brother, you would know he had respect for the law. Your question insults his memory.”

Santo stuttered. “I meant no dis…”

“It was briganti, signuri, for your information…American briganti, who are the worst of all the briganti, so say men who have been everywhere.” He leaned back and snapped at his suspenders, that I believe he did when he was excited, or agitated. “They beat him and robbed him for the change he carried in his pocket. He fought hard, I know, like a man should fight, but in the end, they had guns and shot him in the face,” and he used his finger like the barrel of a gun, pointing it straight at Santo, who was nervous and turned away. “Guns. Over there, they say, every cavone and his brother carries a gun. Let me know if this is true.” He tapped the tabletop, nervously, as his eyes searched the near empty room.

“You say this happened in America?” I asked, to be sure it was not Argentina, or Brazil, or some Spanish country. Was it America? He was slow to answer, fumbling with the rolled-up sleeves on his white shirt, held in place with a band, that he slipped off and threw behind the bar.

“Si. In America. In Bruukland. A pigsty of a place, I have heard. Near the docks, where the marmaglia (rabble) hangout and fester, like here.” He left the table and was gone for some time, or a few minutes, I no longer recall, but his face was red when he returned. I believe he went and hid and cried for his dead brother, and I was glad I did not see him weep, as it is not proper for man, although I have wept, but alone, so I would not lose face in my town. Luca turned to me.

“Should we leave?” Before I could answer Fellino settled himself and wiped his brow with the bar rag.

“No need, my friends. Please stay. I feel better when I talk. But I will apologize no more, for anything, or will I explain myself, or justify what I have done.”

It has been years since that night with Fellino, and I am certain he is long since dead, but I think about him now and then, and for that he is still alive to me. I did not like him, or did I dislike him, but he left me with this feeling it is always better to be healthy in the head, rather than in the body, or the wallet. What good is property and wealth, if you are Fellino.

The side door opened, and two men entered and stood inside the threshold, looking the place over, floor to ceiling. They were too well dressed to be wandering this part of town, let alone to drink in a place such as this. Fellino cut them off before they could choose a table, and he plain lied, flat out, claiming he was closed for business. “Come back tomorrow. and I will wait on you myself and serve you double shots of grappa for the price of one.” They protested, some, that they were thirsty, but double shots of grappa for the price of one was a fine offer, and they agreed they would return the next night.

Fellino despised these people, he said, the big shots, who come down here to visit the grime of Messina, because they have heard stories of whores and cheap wine and grappa. Mostly for the whores, who are decent and work cheap and are good at what they do, so have been told and the cops never complain if the whores are roughed up because whores enjoy it.

Luca asked on the whores re remembered from the old days.

Fellino pounded his chest with a closed fist, “I am no mezzano (pimp).”

Luca was confused. “Then you do not keep no whores here?” Fellino pounded his chest again.

“Are you deaf? I said Fellino is no mezzano”

“I meant no disrespect to you, but I remember…” Fellino stood and pushed his chair with such force it tipped and slid into the next table, that was empty. He righted the chair and mumbled about his temper, that he could no longer control his anger. He pounded the table.

“I forbid any woman to be in here without a proper escort. Except, maybe an old woman no man would want. The whores show up when the Marina Militare is anchored in the harbor, but they pretend to be virtuous women. What can I say? We are in the same business. We sell what we have to sell. I sell grappa and wine. They sell…well, we are men of the world.” He straightened the sleeves on his shirt, that he had rolled up both arm.

“We know what they sell,” he said. I smiled, uncomfortable, though, with talk of lose women, but flattered he saw me as a man of the world. I was not, but what did he know.

“If you men are walking the streets, looking for a pleasant time, let me warn you these women prefer the company of officers.” He starred at Luca and laughed as he snapped a salute.

“But you do not look like men who must pay for it…except, maybe this one,” as he looked direct at Santo, who sunk in the chair. “You are a quiet man,” Fellino said. “I have looked again, and I now believe you are not a man of the world. Let me ask you, when was your last time…if ever?” Luca laughed. I only smiled, as Santo is my cousin. “I am a married man,” he said, in his defense.

Fellino laughed. “A joke, that’s all. Let an old man have his fun….no hard feelings.”

Grazie Dio, he did not ask me, and put me on the spot, as I am no donnaiolo (a womanizer, or cat hound) or would I admit it in front of other men. This talk of whores was uncomfortable and disrespectful to women, and I once swore an oath, I would never have a decent feeling for a dirty woman, and to think of a whore and think of Carmela, was blasphemy. There was a crucifix hanging behind the bar; I looked straight into the face of the crucified Christ, and I could feel my soul turning black. I knew I would burn in hell fire.

I changed the subject, only to save my soul.

“Signuri Fellino. Your brother? How did you get the news? That he was…was dead?” Fellino undid his arms, that were crossed at his chest, and he breathed deep, then grabbed the edge of the table, complaining the paint was tacky and not dry, that he would break the painter’s neck if he has the guts to come and face him, like a man.

“I will tell you about my brother,” he says. “It will remind me of things I should forget, but you are a guest and a customer, and I have led you on with stories.”

A bum, sleeping on a chair, his head against the wall, stirred, then woke and asked for a small glass of wine, to which Fellino said “vai, vai,” and ordered him out of the bar. He got up slowly and stumbled toward the side door, and struggled with the handle, saying he would never return because he was shown no respect. “Vai, vai,” Fellino walked toward the door. “A bum. No more, no less. Good riddance. He buys a glass of wine and thinks I am running a hotel.” He looked back at me. “What was your question?”

“You brother. How did you find out about his death?”

“Si, I was. Giovanni, my oldest brother, wrote the priest in our church, Patri Mancini, also a good customer, except for the women…wine and grappa, only. I remember the day. Patri come in…it was a quiet afternoon…we sat at the bar and drank wine. There was something on his mind I could see, because he always joked, but that day he was gloomy, quiet. I thought, maybe he is ill, from the bad water, or something like that. Then he says, out of nowhere, did I know that God works in strange ways? ‘Yes. You have said it many times,’ I answered, very respectfully. He patted my shoulder, like a good friend, not a priest and says he believes I am a man who can take bad news.
