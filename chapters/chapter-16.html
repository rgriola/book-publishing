He had humbled me.

“What you say…you are right. I will try my best.” He smiled and I believed he respected other men’s humility, but I do not believe he had humility of his own.

“That is what I want to hear,” he said, patting my shoulder. “I joined with you men because you work with your hands and understand privation.”

“Privation? What is it?”

“Privazione.” When he uttered the word, he stretched it and the sound seemed forever. “Priiiivazione, my young friend, is what we have here.” He looked about the room, at the grimy iron floors, the peeling green paint and the thirty, or so, men and women and children packed into a cargo hold, the smoking oil lamps and the fouled air. “This is privation,” he said.

The toilet stink he did not note, but I believed it to be part of ‘privation.’

Luca was annoyed by the talk. “Privation, my ass,” he said.

“When do we eat. Do we live on air for two weeks?”

Two men listened to our talk, Giuseppe Moritti and his son, Micale, from Stacitti, in Calabria, and this made them Calabrese, not Siclianu, but they were proud men, as I could tell. Micale was blind in his right eye, from a bull’s horn, and it was an ugly thing to see when he looked sideways. They were on this very boat before, and Giuseppe remarked it was no better, no cleaner, or the crew more civilized than the first time. But he liked the looks of the women, who were younger and rounder than on the first trip.

Luca was still agitated for the rumbling in his gut. “To hell with these women. What about food? We were promised food when we bought the tickets. Where is it?”

Giuseppe laughed with his son, the bigger fool of the two, and they were like hyena’s the way they carried on together. “You worry about food?” he says to Luca. “Sicilian men I know only worry about their cazzo…and what goat they can fuck.” Giuseppe and Micale laughed like the fools they were, and the son congratulates the father on the good joke he made. Luca moved toward Giuseppe, who was quick and still agile and stepped away. I grabbed Luca’s arm and he calmed down. Giuseppe understood by the look on Luca’s face how close he came to a good beating, and the fool son never said a word or took a step to protect his fool father.

I give this Giuseppe credit, that he saw his error in judgment and tried to ease the tension between him and Luca by changing the subject.

“There is not much food. The better among us will steal some in the next few weeks.”

What there is, he recalled. Was cooked in the middle of the ship, and he guessed it might be put out and any moment, but this was only a guess, as it has been three years since he was on this boat. Luca relaxed, thinking more of his empty gut than beating this fool, Giuseppe. We left the cabin in search of food, while Giuseppe and Micale stayed back, saying they brought a little food and would not eat dog shit until they had no choice. That is what they said, but it was more a fear of Luca, and keeping out of his way.

What I saw was like a religious procession at festa, lines leaving the cabins in search of food. Each man followed the man in front of him, and the hope was that the first man in line knew where he was going. We could have been walking into hell, but what did it matter, if there was food and the food was good, or if not good, there was enough.

Enzo scratched his head.

“This is a problem. They cannot feed us at the same time. I hope there will be no trouble from the hotheads,” and what he said made sense, and it caused me worry. Luca heard it, about the hotheads, and knew Enzo was speaking of him.

“I eat now, not later,” he said, in his course way, loud enough that everyone who knew Luca, knew he would not be put off; a few clapped for him, a few patted his back. One man shook his head, and another hugged him. He was a hero, for nothing more than shooting off his mouth. We ate in a room behind a white iron door, well-guarded by a tall and hefty sailor, mean looking, who seemed German, but spoke decent Italiano. Enzo called him “the centurion,” that sounded vulgar the first time I heard him say it. Luca faced off with the guard, and there was a hundred hungry men at Luca’s back, but he was almost passive, if passive means peaceful, except he stiffened his chest for the benefit of the guard.

“When do we eat,” he demanded of the guard, who glared at Luca and planted his feet, looking him dead in the eye, and neither man flinched or looked away. “Cabin number?” the sailor asked, and he stiffened his chest, and it was a standoff.

“Cabin number, my ass. We come to eat, and we are eating, me and my friends.” I pushed to the front of the line, nudging past a woman who refused to move, cursing me because I touched her body (her arm), but I admitted my mistake, and apologized.

“We are cabin “A” under the water,” I tell the sailor.

“Under the water?” he says. “You mean third class?” and I nod to this.

“Cabin “A” eats now. The rest of you keep the noise down, and be orderly,” he said, leaning around Luca, and shouting to the line, but his orders and manner were not well received, and some men in the line, not from cabin” A,” were agitated and complained.

“There are instructions in the cabins,” the guards shouts, “and if you do not read, find someone who does, because we will not nurse you for the next two weeks.” We protested that all the food would be gone, and some had children to feed. A bit of a man pushed his way to the front and complained the papers in his cabin were written in some foreign language. German, he thought, and they should be written in Italian, but he admitted he could not read any language.

The guard ordered him away and he left without further complaint. Not much of a man, as I could see, to leave after such a small spat and with no satisfaction. Not much of a man.

It was a small room, a ripensamento, an “afterthought,” Enzo believed, as cargo holds do not have dining rooms. It was about the size of our steerage cabin, same green walls, but no peeling paint. I counted ten boxes, used as tables for four, with barely enough room to move, or stretch. The boxes were unpainted, already blessed with a good coat of oil from the air. The nails had not been set and were sharp and rusting from the salt air. The chairs were small boxes and sat poorly and wobbled. The floor was iron, covered with oily wood planks and slippery, covered in a grime I had only seen in an army tool shop. A poor woman slipped as she walked to her table, but a man, her husband, I believe, caught her before she fell. The room smelled and this mattered to me. I complained on the crowding, like an animal pen, and you could not walk a step without bouncing off another human body, and this made the best of us, the gentlest of men, bad-tempered and ready to fight. Women were affected the same way, and this surprised me as they are better in crowds than men, not as easy to push into a fight, since it is in their nature to be gentler, so Enzo said, since nature has charged them with care of the children and a kinder nature was needed for such work. ‘It will get worse,’ I thought, as the days go on, and in a week, we will be enemies, and there will be a war every hour on the hour. Not twelve hours on this ship, I am suffering from boredom, the bad air, and closeness of my fellow man. Maybe poor food, but I hoped it was not the case.
