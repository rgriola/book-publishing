Vermin and the Rule of Survival

The Mess Hall and Basic Needs

It filled the air, the smell of food, the feel of it so thick I thought I could grab it. Good food? We were starving, weak in the knees for the want of food. Anything. Stale bread soaked in olive oil, or no oil, or a small piece of meat, any meat, as long as it was well cooked, and not in a fighting mood when I cut into it. A piece of lamb, but beans were likely, as this place seemed like an army kitchen, small and cramped, everything pushed together to make room for too many. But not as clean as an army kitchen, with a hundred men swing brooms and mops at the snap of the captain’s finger.

An old sailor, hunched over, was cleaning off the tables, pushing the garbage into a small pail hung from his right arm, while talking with himself, sometimes laughing, as he moved in as soon as the eaters left, before new eaters could take their place.

The food kettles sat on a long table against the wall, and it was at the end of this table where the line was formed.

Luca nudged me, “I hold the table…get me food when you go through the line.”

“Near the door if you can. Do not bury me in the corner, with strangers.” We each took a heavy plate (earthenware, I believe) and a fork and spoon, and a small cup, and I dished out mine and some for Luca. Enzo complained his cup was grimy and rusted inside, but he polished it with a cloth he took from his pocket and seemed satisfied.

“We may need these cups to eat when we get to America,” he said.

Santo grabbed a cup. “Eat? From cups? They eat from cups in America?” Enzo laughed, patting Santo’s shoulder, like a cute little boy.

“No. Not eat, Santo…to beg on a corner.”

“Beg?”

“Si, beg. Mendicare.” I laughed. I imagine a coin hitting the bottom of the cup.

“Grazie. You are very kind,” I would say, hoping he would throw in another. I smiled, but he walked away. Then I am beaten by a band of brigands, who roam the streets in America, I have been told. I plead. ‘Take my cup. Do not let them find me with it frozen in my hand, and my family will be shamed, and my name will be like a curse in Sicilia. They push me in the river (some river, I do not know its name) and I thanked them for this courtesy.

“Antonino! Wake up. Are you with us?”

The spoons (no forks) were made of soft, black iron, and most were covered with a fine dusting of rust, so I pick through the bin until I found one clean enough to suit me. The guard (the spoon guard) sees me do it, but he looked the other way. Soft iron was a poor choice for these tools, Enzo said, as they rot from the salt in the air, but they were cheap and cheap is efficient. He stepped aside and let a family of four into the line, and I was pissed that he did it, as it caused me a longer wait before I could get at my food. Luca stood guard over our captured table and chased away two men who saw it as empty and fair game, and they tried to claim it for their own use.

We took our meal from three iron pots. Pot one, some kind of beans, green, soaking in oil, maybe water and oil, but not olive oil. I slopped more than a share onto my plate, thinking “once through the line,” was a true and accurate rumor. Pot two, boiled potatoes, rough quartered, in their skin, and no way appealing to me, but I took more than my share. The army fed potatoes every day, cooked any way possible, but mostly boiled. Men from the north ate them raw, with salt, claiming they were a delicacy, and only foolish men ate them cooked, soft, like something fed to a baby. I saw potatoes in my sleep, as the good crops were poor (no rain), and nothing would grow but potatoes. “Potatoes are like weeds,” papa said. “A cup of dirt will grow a thousand of them, anywhere, even between rocks and stone.” I prayed for meat in the last pot, and He heard me, as the last pot contained an odd-looking meat, maybe, but not lamb. It was boiled down hard, cut into thin strips for better digestion, and no civilized man should eat shaved meat, except during long periods of privation. Even the army does not shave the meat, or I do not recall it, or I did not care at the time. It was old goat, I would bet, without the foul smell, unless the smell was boiled away, and the carcass soaked in vinegar for at least a week. I heaped it,(whatever it was) atop the beans and potatoes, sniffing the plate all the way across the room, like a derelict with a handout. I spooned half onto Luca’s plate, and he never bothered to give me a thanks.

“No drink?” he asked, in a manner you would use talking to a fool. “Water would be good, clean water would be better, but wine would be best, and Siclianu wine would be like the gift of life.”

“Water? Where is the water?” I asked of no one in particular, but there was no water, or wine or wine, or anything to drink. Nothing. Two men and two women shared the next table(box); they finished the meal, and the two men drank wine while their women talked quietly.

Luca wanted a drink and he decided it would be his, and he cleared a path through a crowded room, raising only minor protests, but his stern look alone quieted the worst of the complainers. He grabbed a sailor by the arm, too vigorously, I think, demanding a drink of something, but this sailor was an eager fighter, and he pulled away, and shook a stick in Luca’s face. The two squared off, but there was no room for a real fight, so what was the use, and they talked it over and no punches were thrown. They blame the error (no water)on a new crewman, who did not bring out the barrel because he was not ordered to bring out the barrel, and he refused to do it on his own. Luca backed away and half assed apologized for grabbing the sailor, who was not gracious, or did he accept the apology, and for the next three or four days, he watched us from the corner of his eye. No water, but we were still God damned hungry and ate whatever we could find, like four stray dogs. The talk at the table was the great meat question, as two of us thought it might be, but bad beef. One said pork, but I said no, thinking it was goat, old goat, not lamb as I always enjoyed. Whatever it was in the other life, I am sure it lived a brutal life and was glad to die. We ate it all, cleaning our plates near dry, agreeing it was the worst meal we ever had, or near the worst meal. Once finished, we started to the door, but the sailor who squared off with Luca, blocked our way, his hand in my face. We were told, harshly, as I recall, to take our plates and forks with us, as only one set was allowed each man.

“One set, no more. If you lose them, you eat with your hands, like apes.” “How do we clean them,” I ask.

“Find a barrel,” he said, in the surly voice of authority. “Clean them. That’s what you do.”

Enzo was confused. “Drinking water?” he asked, but politely.

“Used water,” he grunted. “What fool uses clean water?” He was an impatient man, and refused to answer another question, insisting we move on, as he pointed to the door. Enzo complained about “chaos,” and people who filled the room, were not the ‘gentle people,’ he was used to.

The Lifeboat Debate

“And what will happen in an emergency, God forbid? We are already underwater…I am not comfortable with this chaos.” We agreed, it was chaos as he said, and we waited, but nothing changed and then I realize the chaos (caos) he spoke of was a bad thing, to be avoided by all civilized men. Luca points to a young boy and girl sleeping at a table, as the mother and father ate, and he thought children were the smart ones, and should be guiding the mother and father.

My energy gone, I could have slept the rest of the day, but it was sometime before noon, I guessed. My lids were heavy, and I fought to keep awake. Santo sees a board on the wall, and pictures of people climbing into small boats, carrying children who seemed happy and content. It was calm and peaceful in these pictures, but I have not seen any calmness or orderliness as of yet, and there has been no emergency. It was not real life these pictures showed; I imagined what it would be like, the screaming and the clawing for a place in the boat, and to hell with everyone else, including women and children, God help us.

“Where are these boats?” Santo asked me, but I shrugged it off, as I had no answer.

Enzo stood so close to the board, his nose touched it, and I realize he had trouble seeing things, but this he ignored me. “Indeed,” he said. “There should be enough boats for all the crew and passengers.”

Santo agreed. “Thank God for that. I do not want to survive if all of you are dead,” but who believed it, as it is something said but never meant.

Enzo shook his head. “Do not be confident you will die, my good man. I am certain there are a few boats…I saw a few boats…but not enough for every passenger.” Santo puts his hand in the air, like a schoolboy would ask a question.

“Enzo, how is it decided who gets the boat if there are not enough?”

“God has already decided. The biggest and the strongest will have them, or they will do their best to take them.”

“And the rest of us?”

“Morte,” Enzo answered, staring solemn at the floor.

A spit shined officer ordered us to move on.

“No blocking the corridor,” he demanded. We grumble under our breath, but obeyed and walked away, stopping when he disappeared into the dining hall. Enzo scratched at the stubble on his chin.

“What were we talking about? Yes, the boats.” He thought. “I am familiar with the law of the sea, and it demands that women and children are always first into the boats, since they are the weakest. Then men, if there is room, and in some cases, a few will be chosen by the officers. I understand this from my reading, and, of course, the crew will be last, except the men who will steer and operate the lifeboats. The captain stays until the very end, and it is expected, if he must, that he will go down with the ship. It is the honorable thing to do.” Luca stares at Enzo.

“My ass,” he said, grabbing his own. “When is the boss ever been last?”

Santo’s face was white, and his mouth hung open. Except for the captain and the crew, who should be last, he did not think it fair that it is only by accident we are men, and for this we are third in line to be saved, after the women and children? “Not fair, at all,” he said.

To the rule, that the crew would be last, Luca laughed. “This scurvy looking crew of brigands will most likely throw the children overboard, as they are the weakest and easily beaten.”

“You are such a cynical man.” Enzo said.

“Cynical (cinico)” I have not heard this word before.

Luca then disrespected the law of the sea, saying he would not obey it if the boat started to sink. He pointed to the floor, “If we go down…everybody for themselves. Any man who wants my seat will have to take it away from me with a bigger gun,” and at that he patted his rear pocket, the place he carried the pistol, but adding, he would not take a seat from a friend, or any old man, or any child, and not from a woman, but to this he would not swear.

Santo made the sign of the cross. Enzo was shook by what he heard.

“My God, where is the chivalry? When I was young, any man would gladly give his life to save a woman.”

“Bullshit!” Luca said, loud, and anyone in the corridor looked, but was he embarrassed?

He grabbed Enzo by the sleeve.

“When you look death in the eye, how far will you go to save yourself? How immoral will you be?” Enzo stared.

A fair question, I thought, surprised Luca would say it.

Finding the Card Room

How far would I go to save my worthless ass? If I could answer this question, what else was there be to know about myself? Would I beat a woman, or run a wagon over a helpless child? If I did either, how long would it be before I forgot the truth of what happened and start telling the story in a way it made me seem a hero? A young woman, who was carrying a child (a boy) shifted him to her left arm, making the sign of the cross with her right hand. There was a silence, except for the noise of the engines. Santo cleared his throat.

“Some men would trample their mothers…the bastards,” and a young woman catching some of what was said, looked away.

“We are all afraid,” I said, and Enzo agreed with me. “Yes, it is true. All chaotic. Very chaotic.”

Disagreements and fights, something I have always avoided, like death, my whole life. I am not a coward, as it might seem to another man who does not know me, or thinks I am too cautious, like an old man because of his age. Mio amico, Luca Ponto, on the other hand, enjoys the battle, the smell of sweat and fear, and me, I would rather watch it from a hilltop, or running east, if the battle is moving west. This is cowardly only if you are fighting for yourself and have started the battle at a time and place of your own choosing and you are free to leave at will. A soldier cannot be a coward as he is not free to leave. This I believe to be true, like night follows day.

Four rowdy men pushed their way down the corridor to where we stood, and stopped and waited for us to move, and finally the biggest and toughest looking made it an order. This boat, as I can tell, is full of rude and angry people. It seemed these men moved at us on order of a low and common sailor who wore a band on his right arm, with writing I could not read. We were not blocking the way, as he said, but in any case, we submitted, like good contadini, and Enzo was right and correct in this advice. Even the fighter, Luca, moved without argument.

Life is easier, better, if you are obedient, but you will always think of yourself as shit. How to pass the time? Walk the halls? Get our asses kicked by every sailor and officer. “Move on. You are in the way,” or “head back to your beds.”

A man, who smelled of fuel oil, with streaks of thick grease on his face, stood at the end of this particular corridor, a small cigar hanging from his mouth. Santo asked him, respectfully, “is there a place we can talk or play cards?” He coughed and wiped his nose with his sleeve.

“Si, there is. Up one deck, then go aft, not forward.” At the back of the boat, he explained, we would find a room where we can sit, play cards, or drink wine if we had it. A good place to be lazy, he said, in the way I believed he enjoyed idleness, that is comfortable for some, but too much is a vice, an evil, like sin, to be avoided. The benefit to idleness is that there is no need for a boss, and maybe just two weeks of freedom from these bastards, might mean the beginning of a new life. I will enjoy this freedom, if it is freedom, and if it is not freedom, then what is the harm? No capu lording over my shoulder, taunting me, “kiss my ass, young man, to keep your job, and the stable were you and your family sleep. More work, give me more work, or you are out.” This is heaven, or as close to heaven as I will get. I grew to be a man, never lingering, or being idle, with no time to call my own, “Relax when you die,” papa said, but he smiled. “Now I have work for you to do when you have finished work for the day.” If he was standing here, what would I say to papa’s face? He is the pattern from which I was made. We are each other; the same blood, the same thought on most everything.

I am too sentimental and sometimes think like a woman, to my embarrassment. I hear my name. “Antonino, where are you? Somewhere else?”

Half the boat is ‘Antonino.’ Maybe I am daydreaming. “No…I am here. Where else would I be?”

We climbed the narrow stairs, iron stairs, not wood stairs, wide enough for one man, and we fight against a line of people coming down at the same time. At the top we loitered, wondering where we were. Enzo read aloud a small and faded sign. “A destra,” he said, plainly. My new rule: only follow men who can read the signs.

“We have landed in second class,” Enzo said. “Yes. I am sure. This is second class.”

The floors were clean, and the air smelled of fresh paint and the walls were brushed smooth, no runs, no streaks, no sags. Through an open door, we see a small room for two or three people, or a small family, not a common room, like a barracks or a meeting hall. A second class room. Second class is better than steerage, but not as good as first class. Lucky bastards are people who travel in Second Class, but not as lucky as people who travel in First Class. We were afraid of being where we were; second class, when only we paid for third class, so we moved on toward the end of the ship, in a long and narrow hallway. A woman in the middle years, a contadina by her look and manners, was talking to a prosperous young man, but they were not together. She was lost, to be sure, and confused, and he said, politely, and pointing to the stairs, “third class is one deck down.” His dialect was strong, from il nord, I believe, and I wondered why he came South to take a boat. Distances cannot be judged in such poor light, but I guess we walked, maybe, two hundred feet, all together, in this passageway, not more than three feet wide. People coming from where we were going, kicked and elbowed, because we were in the spot they wanted to be. Expect an elbow in the eye, or a kick in the leg, or higher. We found the room that was described to us, and Enzo claimed to be familiar with these places, as he taught school in England, and his hotel had a room, where only men were allowed, and they played cards and drank wine and grappa, and told stories not fit to be told in front of women, or within earshot. Papa would not approve of such things.

“We drank hard and cursed often,” Enzo said, “but I enjoyed my time.”

Enzo? Curse? I did the same things he mentioned, the drinking, the stories, some cursing, but in the piazza after work, maybe Sunday mass, if I went, and there were no women, who were mostly at home preparing the meal and cleaning.

This room was wide and high, about thirty feet long and the width of the ship at the rear. But these dimensions I guessed at, by pacing off the steps, three feet to each pace, as this was the way to measure in Sicilia. Two cabins would fit easily between the walls, and we thought it was decent to give up paying space, so we could play cards and drink wine, or grappa if we had it, as I do not believe it was sold on the boat. A bare room, except for tables and chairs, not like the room where we were fed, and forced to sit on empty boxes. These tables were round and efficient, as more men can be sat at a round table. Oak, but unpainted, with four or five chairs each, laid out in no ordered way. A short length of rope was nailed to the underside of each table, then passed through an iron eye, bolted to the floor, as a good way to keep things from moving in bad weather. The room was near empty, to my surprise; only four men playing cards, drinking and making some noise, while at a separate table four women waited, talking amongst themselves. I did not expect women, as they should not be allowed in this area, but given their own room where they could watch the children. A sailor, no more than a boy, neatly stacked boxes in a corner of the room, each box in line with the box below it. His neatness and attention to work impressed Enzo.

“Military precision,” he declared.

“He takes pride, even the simple task of stacking boxes.” Me? I did not see the beauty of it.

“Stacking boxes is not art, but in a way, it is a lost art,” he said.

A board hung on the wall, near hidden by a pile of ten poorly stacked boxes What use did I have for words, if I could not read?

Enzo, always curious, inspected the board, his nose sometimes touching the paper, and I understood could not see good, and prayed this was the worst of his infirmities.

“What does it say?” I ask.

“This notice here, let’s see,” he pointed to the picture of a boat, “it is our…our…let’s see…our estimated arrival in America.” He squinted, then turned his head sideways, stepped back, then closer. “Yes. That’s it. And I see these notes are written in long hand by someone,” he paused and scratched at his chin. “By someone schooled in Genova. I can see by the slant of the letters.” The board was set back in the shadows, and Enzo stepped aside and squinted his eyes. “Yes, definitely, I recognize it as Genovan…Genovan. I am certain of it.” He took his pair of well-worn spectacles from his pocket, round lenses, trimmed in brass, with a bent bow that he straightened it. He breathed on the lenses, wiped them dry with a small handkerchief, and fumbled as he struggled to set them precisely on the bridge of his nose. They hung too far down, as I could see, and he tilted his head back to focus on the board. He shook his head.

“Shameful scribbling, and the penmanship is disgraceful…these Genovans write like ignorant contadini,” and when he said it, I was insulted, but let it pass, as I was not certain who he meant. He slowly turned and stared from the corner of his eye, pushing the spectacles up the bridge of his nose, from where they slipped again. He cleared his throat.

“Chiedo scusa. My apologies to all of you. Of course I did not mean you men, but I fear it sounded that way.” He turned back to the board and continued to read, and he made no other mention of ‘ignorant contadini.’

“It says,” and he squinted and moved closer, “it says we will arrive at New York Harbor in fourteen days, if all goes well. Two weeks. Maybe three with bad weather.” He cleaned the lens, again, with his jacket sleeve, saying plainly, he needed a new pair, as they were weak and old and the lens were scratched, and the bows were bent too many times. He gazed at the board, as if in a trance.

“Very difficult to read with bad spectacles,” he said, as he moved in another step.

“But we should land March 7th. About three in the afternoon if these people are correct.” Santo tapped his finger on the board, “So we dock…what then Enzo?”

“What then? Then we shall see what all the fuss is about.” “And where do we go?”

“Where we like.”

I punch Santo’s arm, gently, not to make him angry. “Did you forget our plan?”

“I did not forget,” he said. “Am I stupdio?” And for show, no other reason, he repeats the plan we made months ago.

“We get off the boat and find the train to Siracusa. We meet up with cousin, Natale, if he shows, because he has promised us work on the steam road. The three of us, we save our money, live on shit, like back home. In a few years we will have money…we go home and buy our own land…be our own capu. But if this place does not want us, we say va' fa' un culo to all of you,” and leave the next day. He smiled, thinking he had won, proving he was no stupido.

“Did I pass?”

“Si, you did,” and together we smiled, and it was at that moment I remember my promise to Carmela, that I would call for her if America was to my liking and she would come and my family will come. Why go back, with a pocket full of lire and great thoughts, when the stronger men, the men with more lira and bigger thoughts, will steal what I have? Like a hundred years ago and no different.

Santo wiped the sweat off his hands on the front of his jacket. “I am worried about Natale. What if he does not show? What then? It has been months since we heard from him.” What if…what if…what if?

Enzo took his arm. “Too many ifs, but in that case we will all swim back to Sicilia.” We laughed, except Santo, who has never laughed in his life.

“You are foolish people,” he said, and turned away.

Enzo lifted the spectacles from his nose and took another step closer to the board.

“Listen to this, my friends” He squinted at the board. “German Maritime Law demands this ship carry twenty lifeboats, or sometimes more.”

“Did you read the rule correct,” I ask. He did and it made no sense.

“Twenty lifeboats for more than a thousand?” He agreed it was not enough, and many might drown, and that would be a tragedy, but Enzo was no professional seaman, with no education or special training in these matters.

Only twenty boats? Luca was confused.

“I saw, maybe, ten, and one had a hole in the side the size of Santo’s head. Another had a missing plank. That is eight.”

Enzo scratches his chin.

“This does not seem right. My friends, we have a complaint, of sorts. Not enough boats, and at least two need major repair.” He cleared his throat. “Legitimate grievances, I believe.”

We heard later, from some ‘salt’ as they like to call themselves, there was a method to the calculation, that if this boat goes down, many will drown and have no need for a lifeboat, leaving the rest for the living. He was an old fool.

Enzo gets in close, at the bottom of the board, and reads the very small print, underneath the small print.

“It says plainly, we are allowed to complain to the “German Maritime Ministry” in Berlin. I shall write them in the morning.” He said this foolishly, as a joke, and we all laughed, but not Santo. We would have laughed at anything, short of our own pain, but certainly the pain of others. No. Not our own pain. I am certain of it. And, as I say, Santo never laughs, and Luca, only sometime. Me? I laugh. What do I know?

A man stood in a group of men playing cards, shouts from across the room. “What is the joke, my friend? Tell us. We can use a laugh over here.”

So, Enzo read the notice again, now with vigor, feeling he was on display and the men playing cards desired their entertainment. They laughed as they raised their cups and praised Berlin. They stood and one offered a toast, that all on the boat will arrive in America safely and he promised he would pray to San Rocco and say a Novena every week for a year, at the great Catholic Church in New York City, that he did not name, but Enzo said he meant Saint Patricks Cathedral. The man admitted fearing for his family, but regrettably he brought no stamps for a letter of complaint, should the boat sink to the bottom of the sea. We laughed and applauded his joke, and he turned red from the attention and maybe too much wine. These men were four farmers from Calabria, out of work, with nothing to lose, and decided one night they would try America for a while. They shared what they felt, and one wept, confessing he left them all behind, and would say nothing more about it. They did not offer to share their wine, but when they had all finished their drink, each waved, said ci vediamo, and left the room. The last man, the man who wept in front of other men, staggered and I thought of offering a hand, but out of respect, I did not.

The energy from the joking and the stories told was gone, and a great fatigue se. I could barely stand, as my legs wobbled as they sometimes do on a drunk. We took a table and tried to relax, watching people wander through the door, alone, by two’s, whole families, looking for a good seat to set in and pass the time. A good seat is important in these things, and what is better than relaxing when there is nothing to do, and you are spared that guilty feeling of laziness.

Fourteen days of this, or more. God help me, I have not been idle fourteen days since I learned to walk. Is it natural and good, or is it Blasphemy?

A man alone was leaning against the wall, for almost an hour, and seemed embarrassed he had nothing to do, and for this he played with his fingers, like the crazy man back home, Giuseppe Monti, who stood all day in the piazza (but only in good weather) playing with his fingers, saying kind things to everyone who passed.

This room were found, a room made for drinking wine and grappa (but they did not serve it) and playing cards, and cursing, yet women and children were allowed. A well-lit room, by eight oil lamps in all, but two were near empty and smoking, sending ribbons of soot into the air. Two men sat quietly at a small corner table, smoking cigars and the smoke, God damn them, floated across our table, and I started coughing and slapping at my chest. They stopped smoking and butted their cigars, but soon they were smoking again.

What man makes another man sick and will not see the wrong in it?

I asked Luca if this particular room was below the water line? He said plainly, without giving it much thought, why would he care, as long as the walls and ceiling and floor did not leak. Instead of being concerned, as I was, he took a deck of cards from his shirt pocket and pushed it across the table. “We should play some cards, no?”

“Cards? “I would rather hide in the dark, like some child, than play games right now,” Luca slapped the table, and it shook, as the legs were loose. He looked square at me, shaking his head.

“Playing cards, my friend, is how Sicilian men have passed the time for a thousand year.” No matter. I saw it as a waste of life, nothing more. Better a man runs in circles, like a crazy dog trying to catch his tail. Or small talk, and I have always hated small talk. But to Luca, to my father, most men, a card game is like war, but without the risks and the blood and the death. War with rules everyone must obey, that a civilized man can believe in and feel good about, with no chance to suffer, except for what he loses and any debt he might make to the winner. This, they say, is what makes cards a good and honorable pastime. But that was then, I was a child, but my opinions have changed since the days I played pitch and briscola and saw them as games children play. Today is the second day; tonight, will be the second night, but it is really the first day, as we came on the boat after midnight. First day, second day; I miss feeling the heat of sun, so hot it can burn the eyes and sear the flesh.

Santo brooded, complaining he should not have left his wife behind, and as God as his judge, he missed her. He has always believed he suffers alone, and that makes him feel good in some way. Luca just sat and watched, lusting after every woman who walked by, yet none were as beautiful as the women back home, so he said. Not one. Enzo, the teacher, counseled (his word) that we make the best of this idle time. I ask myself for the hundredth time, am I doing the right thing? Will there be a better place than here? Many go over and come back, cursing America. Is it right I left my family to their own? I felt sick upstairs, in my head. We stood four grown men, weeping together, and today is worse than yesterday. I left them to wander the boat, getting lost in a maze of corridors and hallways. I came across a man sitting on a step, praying the “Our Father,” and I believe he was crying, but he looked away and I could not swear for certain. He stood, excused himself for blocking the stairs. He was Giuseppe…I cannot recall his last name…from Castellemmare del Golfo, Sicilia, in the west of my country.

“I was praying when you came on me,” he said. “To our patron, San Rocco, for peace and guidance.” After a time of small talk, he passed on a rumor he heard from a man in his cabin, who claimed it came from a crewman, that steerage would not be allowed on deck during the trip across.

“For our safety,” he said, and scoffed. “My ass…we will die down here.”

“But they promised,” I said. “We would be allowed on deck for sun and fresh air.” “They said this to your face, or you heard it and it is bad information?”
