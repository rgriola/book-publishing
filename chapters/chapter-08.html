Chapter 8

The Beggar, the Mule, and the Hundred-Mile Walk

How far to Messina? Hours, yet. But miles. How many miles? The great trees that seemed to reach heaven and hide the sea. But I knew it was there, the sea; I heard it and smelled it and felt the greasy salt grit on my face, but I could not see it. I have said this before, that I love the sea and will live next to the sea in America. For now, I worry about these great trees and the shelter they give the briganti, where they hide in wait. Briganti…all murdering bastards.

Luca yelled so the dead could hear.

“Antonino…up ahead…a rider…a rifle across his shoulder.” Santo, hear the warning and saw the rider with his own eyes, edged toward the woods, until Luca raised his right hand and laughed.

“Relax,” he said, and Santo stopped cold. “An old man on a mule. His rifle is a crooked stick…he is harmless.” Santo breathed deep and picked himself a broad oak and pissed on it. He sang some song I did not know, about a pleasant summer day in the hills. “I thought so,” he said when he finished. “The way he sits on the mule. An old man. Easy to see.”

He was a very old man, seventy or eighty, leaning so far forward I thought he might fall off before he got to where we stood, as he looked to be in bad shape, and his mule, old, almost a swayback. The good years were miles behind both of them. The old man pulled and cursed, and the mule stopped a few feet short of where we stood, and we circled them in the middle of the road. The old man’s eyes were black and hollow, and he stared in a daze. Was he almost dead and frozen to the back of this old mule?

He snapped free of the daze and looked us over.

“Bongiornu,” he said, wiping his nose with the sleeve of his shirt. “Do you men know the name of this town? I have not been here before.” He smelled like fresh garbage, festering in the July sun. I was closest and took his full broadside when the wind changed. I turned and coughed, tasting the rush of bile running up my throat.

“Where are you men going?” he asked. His head trembled; his hands shook so bad he finally hid them behind his back. “Where are you men going?” he wanted to know? Tell no one your business, my grandfather says; say nothing, not even to a foul smelling, harmless old man, and his dilapidated mule. How could I disrespect him for his age?

“Messina,” I said, pointing north, offering nothing else. Was it his business?

He was straccione, wearing rags, not even clean rags, but filthy rags that cried for soap and water and a washboard, if they could survive a good scrubbing. He was uncomfortable, I see, fidgeting with the waist band of his pantaloni, that was tightly cinched with a thin rope. The pants were old and threadbare; his knees, all bone and no flesh, popped through the holes, and looked like ugly tumors attached to a frail body. He was a beggar, no doubt; an old, old beggar who counted his time not in years or months, but days. Likewise, his mule, suffered with bad legs, all four, and a near hairless hide. “Swayback” means old and soft, with bones that bend and crumble; a body with little time left. The poor bastard struggled to breathe, even at rest, coughing like a human, that sent a chill down my back.

“What’s the stick for, signuri?” Luca asked.

The old man was hard of hearing, or near blind (or both), as he could not tell who asked the question. He always stared at Santo and nodded when Luca talked.

“Stick? What stick?”

“The stick you carry across your shoulders, like a rifle.”

“Oh, the stick.” He laughed. “I keep it for the god damned dogs. I crack their skulls with it, if they get too close,” and he said it proudly, as if it made him a warrior. He groaned and leaned and pointed to the mule’s front legs. “These bastard dogs are wild. They nip his legs and he near threw me more than once along the way. So, I bash their heads good. If I get one square,” he points to his head, “you can hear their yelp and pain in Roma. It makes my day worthy.” He pointed up the road, to Messina. “I laid a few heads open about a mile back…did you hear it?” I shake my head, no.

“I am sorry, signuri. It has been quiet, like a funeral.”

He asked, politely, could he sit with us, as he has not seen people for a day, maybe more. “Give me a hand down. Nothing my mother gave me works right anymore.” He whines the words; not manly, not even at his age. Luca gives him a hand, and the old man lets out a loud grunt when his feet hit the ground. Like a dwarf he was, maybe five feet tall; no doubt taller and broader in his younger days, but as I could tell, he weighed nowhere near a hundred pounds. Sickly and filthy, smelling worse than an old dog, were poor things to carry together. What does it take to find a stream, or a ditch with water? His long silver hair was tangled and knotted and hung far over his collar. Even in the dimming light I see his green eyes were very green, and his nose far more Roman than Siclianu, too long and wide for his wasted face. Maybe in his youth, they fit well together, but no more.

“Grazie,” he says, bowing slightly at the waist, like a man of means would do.

“Beg your pardon for the stink, but I have been stuck to the back of this damned mule for most of two days maybe three. I am not certain.” He curls his lip. “Old men lose track of time. I am sure I smell more like a mule than a man, and for this I apologize.” He laughed, but it was true; the both of them smelled like rotting garbage in the July sun. I repeat myself, yes, but what other way can I tell the agony of being down wind of these two. “Rotting garbage in the July sun.” I moved to his other side, as the wind changed, and now I was up wind of him.

“We can find a clearing in the woods,” I said. “If you want to sit and rest.”

“Rest? No. Already wasting too much time. I need to walk off these aches and pains.” He locked his hands tightly, stretching his arms high over his head. “Ah, that feels good,” he says, and I hear sounds, like breaking twigs, from over his body, mostly his joints. Never have I seen a man plain fall apart in front of me. He had no poke as I see no bulge in his pockets, or did he carry saddlebags and I knew it right off he was a beggar. Old man, or not, we had nothing to spare, no food, no water, no patience.

“You live around here?” I asked. He looked at the woods, the sky and seemed confused.

“No, I do not believe so. I wish I did, but I think I live many miles from here.” He pulled a rag from his sleeve, like a woman, and dabbed at the sweat on his forehead and neck, while complaining the heat was bad for this time of year. “I hope to make it home before one of us is dead. Once I would have prayed for this beast to drop dead first, but now what do I care.” He slapped it on the ass, but lightly; the mule moved side to side, but otherwise stayed in place. “I am from Aci Catena, somewhere around here. Have you men heard of it? A small town. Not many know it, since what it there to speak of. Empty, like the bottom of a hole. A few worked out farms, a little fishing on the coast, but thanks to God the mountains are covered with chestnut trees, except Etna. Castagne. I love castange(chestnuts).” He paused to stretch. “Have you seen the chestnut trees on the mountains? A grand and beautiful sight, the way they…”

Luca grew impatient by the old man’s rambling.

“Si, Aci Catena. I know it. South, near Catania.”

“You have been there?”

Luca points to the south. “Si, in the army. Garrisoned at Catania for a month. Maybe sixty or seventy miles from here. A four or five-day ride. Can you make it?”

The old man scratched. “I made it this far,” he said, wiping the sweat from his forehead. “If I do not make it all the way, I pray a kind person will bury me off the side of the road, but in the shade…I cannot stand this god damnable heat.”

I step closer to the mule, and it bucked, but the old man grabbed the reins and pulled hard and talked to it and it settled down. I have seen a nervous mule kick a man’s head to pieces.

“Why are you so far from home?” I ask. He thought.

“Home? Why am I so far from home? Is that what you want to know?” He thought. “Oh, si, I went to visit my brother…one last time, I figured.” He lowered his head and scuffed the road with the toe of his boot. “My brother and me. We are the last of our family. The others, all dead and buried. God rest their souls.” He bowed his head, as he made the sign of the cross. “Three sisters and two brothers, but now, everyone is gone except me, and I can say it, the last brother was my favorite, and I will miss him the most.”

Santo follows the old man and makes the sign of the cross.

“Did he live far from here?” I asked.

“Si, si. On a farm, but not much of a farm. Its only value is that it lies on the slope of a hill near Patti, overlooking the sea. The soil is rocky, and the crops usually failed and my brother lived day to day, but he could never leave. All the great beauty. I do not blame him.” He paused. “Did I say his farm overlooks the sea?”

I nodded. “Si, you did.”

“‘My God it is beautiful here,’ I say to my brother, ‘you are a lucky man,’ but he said, ‘how lucky is a man who outlives his children and watches them all die.’ I understood what he said, in a way, though I have not been blessed with children. But I knew in my gut it was true.

“Do you men have children?” he asked. “The great regret of my life is that there is no man to carry on my name…it dies with me.”

“No. We have no children among us,” I said. “Only Santo is married.” Santo smiled and wanted to talk about his wife, *Agata Rossi, but the old man cut him short.

He points to his mule. “Only have boys,” he said. “Do not waste your time with girls. And do not wait too long to marry, or this is what you will bed down with at night.” We laughed, but Luca, who was the best with women, seemed confused. He took a strong step toward the old man, who moved back, quick.

“This trip you took. Are you saying you rode this ungodly beast, maybe two hundred miles, up and back to your brothers?” The old man laughed.

“Ungodly beast? Yes, it is an ungodly beast. But no. This poor bastard could not make the trip you describe.” He wiped his forehead, complaining about the heat, as he run the rag down the back of his neck. “I walked. Signuri. From my shack in Aci Catena, to Patti up north.”

Luca stepped back and laughed. “I do not believe you. It’s a hundred miles. Maybe more…and you say you walked?”

“Si, at least a hundred. Who knows?” He stared at Luca, “and why is the distance important to you?” Luca shook his head.

“No disrespect meant to you, signuri. But I look at you, I do not see a man who could not walk ten miles and survive. No. I do not believe it.” The old man shook his head.

“It happened. But I admit the walk near put me down. I was only a gasp, a breath away from meeting the grim reaper when I get to the bottom of the hill, and I could see my brother’s farmhouse. I was reborn and for the last quarter mile I was a young man again. Thank God I did not have to walk back but was blessed with a gift of this creature… I worry on its legs…the legs are bad.” Now I look better at the old mule, and I see its legs were swollen up and twisted, one joint twice the size of the other.

“Do you know these beasts?” he asked me. “It is all legs with these creatures, as you know if you worked the land. The legs say this old pile of bones has little time left. It was my brother’s mule, and his wife did not believe I would make it back home by foot, so she asked…no she insisted, I take it, as a gift. She warned it would not make it all the way to Aci Catena.” He grabbed at the frayed lapels of his coat, “but I knew it would, and it has done me right, at least until now, and I respect it because it is a creature from God.”

“You respect it?” Santo asked. “You feed it and wash it down each night?”

“No. Nothing like that. This beast eats grass, and if it cannot not find grass, it does not eat. Who worries about me? And what fool washes down a mule? A waste of life, as I see it.”

“You respect it?” Luca asked. “You rode it into the ground…this animal is half dead.”

The old man stroked the swayback, and the mule turned and looked at him.

“I give it a name. It has never had a name before, just ‘mule.’ Now he answers to *Peppino. Peppino, the mule. And I never call him ‘it,’ although my brother had him gelded, but ‘he’ is what God meant him to be. ‘Peppino, you are doing fine. Peppino, only a few more miles.’ I will be sorry if Peppino dies with me on its back…yet it is the nature of things…but he will die with his own name.”

“And your brother?” I asked. “If I am not being too curious.”

The old man stared at the ground and sighed.

“Dead. In his bed. A week, or so, ago. God rest his soul,” and he made the sign of the cross and Santo, listening close, made the sign, but Luca did not, and I did not.

“God rest his soul,” said the old man, bowing his head. “I am certain God welcomed him through the gates of heaven. I hope to see him again someday.” It was an awkward time, in the middle of the road with a man and his mule, wondering what to say to a stranger about the death of his brother, who we have not met? What kind of a man was he, his brother, and what good or bad did he do? What do you say? We said nothing, or did we offer our sympathies or say something that would make him feel better. Now I see, looking back on all of this, that we were wrong to ignore him. Luca shook head.

“You say you walked all the way just to see your brother? I do not believe it.”

“I did. I would have walked in the front door of hell to see him. He is the last of my mother’s children, except for me. As you can see, I am still here.” Santo took the old man’s arm and they faced each other.

“How long did you walk? Did you see any briganti?”

“Two, weeks, or longer, maybe. I was often lost, but what is time? Why worry about it?”

A small animal, maybe a rat or a squirrel, runs across the road and the mule reared and near pulled out the old man’s arm. I see he was suffering, as he rubbed his shoulder, but he never complained.

“As I was saying…si…I only walked in the daylight. At dusk, I stopped and looked for food, sleeping in the woods, or in a barn if I thought the owner would not shoot me between the eyes.”

“Were you afraid?” Santo asked. “Did you run into briganti?” he asked again.

“Afraid? Afraid of what? Do I look rich, or dangerous? What fool would think he could buy a chunk of rancid cheese with the coin I have in my pocket? It is a waste of good sweat to rob me.”

Santo laughed. “You are right. Who would waste their time robbing you? Not me.”

I looked close at his possessions. A worthless mule. No money, he claimed (but we all say it). Not even his boots were worth the effort; they were leather, but cracked and rotting and the stitching was frayed, and the sole of his left boot flopped, foolishly, when he walked. In all, including his worthless mule, what he had showing, his boots and the cap, worth maybe a lira, not enough for one brigante to get drunk on cheap wine, or watered-down grappa.

“How did you eat?” Luca asked. “What did you eat?”

“Eat? Well. Let me see. When I left home, I put what little food I had in a pack, slung it over my shoulder and started walking. When the food was gone, well, I begged. Am I ashamed of it? I am not.” He pointed at each of us, me last, “and you men would have done the same.” He arched his back and stretched his arms, said he was feeling better and thanked us for being on the road, or he would not have stopped or rested, maybe dying on his mule and they would both wander until the mule died, or threw him off.

“But you seem to be a proud man,” Luca said. “Begging? You are not ashamed to beg?”

The old man shook his head, no.

“I am ashamed for my sins, not of being hungry. This is my first-time begging, and what are the rules? Am I supposed to be ashamed? Is it the law? You see, I figured when my food was gone, I would find a farmer, one not too rich, not too poor.” He wiggles his right hand, like a butterfly’s fluttering wings. “Metà, a metà…I wanted somewhere in the middle.” He coughed and the mule reared. “These poor farmers, I reasoned, have nothing to give, and the rich would want too much for stale bread and rancid cheese…stale bread and rancid cheese…like gold to these bastards.” The old man grabbed what he could of his thin waist. “It worked for me, but not always. I went hungry a few times, or I ate berries off the side of the road or stole fruit. Who misses a little fruit, a pear, an apple? If I was starving hungry, I would look pitiful, making sure they saw the holes in my pants, or I would tell them Jesus’ wisdom, ‘What you do for the least of mine, you do for Me.’ These words worked for me, except when I stopped at the rectory in Villafranca, and the priest said he did not recall these words I claimed were said by Jesus, and he insisted I work the day for a piece of stale bread and two sardines.”

Luca laughed (he hated priests). “Then you did his work for him. The lazy sonofabitch.”

The old man agreed that priests were mostly lazy men who lived off the poor and the weak. And not all are intelligent men, as he told the story of a priest near Mongiove, “a simpleton, but a better Christian than most, as he fed me first.” He bowed his head and kicked at a loose stone in the road. “I admit eating his bread and sardines, and when his back was turned, I ran off, telling myself I will pay for my sin on the way back, and work off the day as promised. Maybe giving him an extra days work to soothe my guilt. But on the way home…with this mule and all, I was ashamed to visit the priest, afraid he might want to feed us both, and he had little food to spare when I ran out on him. It would not be right.” Santo cleared his throat.

“Signuri? May I ask you a question?”

“I have nothing to hide.”

“Did you see your brother, before he…?”

“Died? You may say it. Si, I did, and I thank you for your kindness. I saw him. He passed a few days after I arrived. God rest his wonderful soul.” His eyes teared and we turned away so as not to see it and embarrass him, but he straightened himself and apologized for this show of ‘weakness.’ He said ‘weakness.’ I did not say ‘weakness.’ I saw no weakness.

“Did he talk?” I asked, thinking of my grandmother, who was left behind. “You spoke with him?” He wiped at his eyes with the sleeve of his shirt, while asking for patience, if he seemed too much like a woman.

“We spoke, yes, for what it was worth. He was weak, and near the end, just staring at me. I held his hand, at night, and he talked to me with lips, but no words.

‘I love you, brother,’ I know he said, repeating it a hundred times over. His voice was gone, but there was strength in his look, and I could see it in his eyes, that he was not afraid to die, and thank God for that. What could be worse than the fear of death at that moment; could the pain be worse? When we were young, we argued, who was the better man? As you plainly see, I am not much of a man, not brave like my brother, mostly a coward all my life. Near the very end he squeezed my hand, as hard as he could, for as long as he could; and I know him, I know what he was saying, that he was still the better man. And he was, even as he lay dying.” At this the old man rested and caught his breath and settled his emotions. “My brother was born at midnight, and he passed at midnight, and I held his hand until morning, when I buried him on the hill that looks at the sea.” He turned away again; the tears, I could see, were falling down his cheeks, like rain. “He was always the stronger man, the better man and I always looked to him for help and strength. This is the first time I have cried for my brother. Forgive me. I do not know you people, or do you know me.” I was glad he turned away, as it is my thought it is wrong for a man to weep in front of other men, no matter the reason. I took his arm, but he pulled away. We left him in the road, weeping, and walked to a clearing in the woods and sat on the downed trunk of an old oak. In a few minutes’ time he joined us, saying he did not want to be alone, and for his own reason he picked a rock covered with moss, but shaped like a chair, and he settled on it. Seeing his grief, that he wept like a woman, made me uneasy. More, that I knew nothing about him, not even his name.

“You are a lucky, in a way,” I said. “You had time with your brother, like the old days…when you were young.”

He smiled. “We were all born there, on papa’s farm. A long time ago.” He sighed. “Si, a long time ago.”

“Then you have good memories, that are worth something, even for the long journey.”

“Good memories, yes. When we talked, he could only talk with his eyes and a slight nod of his head. I talked about the old grey neither of us could ride. Crazy wild, it was, and always bucked us both to the ground, on our asses, more than once.” He laughed, and looked back at his mule, still in the middle of the road. “This foul animal, my cognata(sister-in-law) swears, is a grandson of the very grey we talked about. Can this be possible? Papa’s grey was a majestic animal, that a king would ride. A wild one that showed up one day and lived for thirty more years. I refuse to believe this old swayback comes from the grey…never. But it is a good story.” Luca laughed.

The old man nodded, he agreed. “Si, but this pathetic creature has served me well, so far, anyway. Maybe tomorrow it will die with me on its back, or I will die with it under me.”

“So, your family is all gone?” I ask.

“Si, you are correct. Papa first, and mama a few years later. That was the worst time. I have been empty, like a hole, since that she died. I believe it was thirty, thirty-five years ago, or something like that. I left after she passed and have been back only a few times.” Luca touched the old man’s shoulder, softly, like a woman, and his show of warmth surprised me, as I had not seen this decent quality in him before, or since that day.

“Where do you go from here?” Santo asked.

The old man scratched at the stubble on his face, as he did whenever we asked a question.

“I have nothing now, or no one, so I head home and wait to die. I imagine he might be there, waiting, when I walk through the door. What else can an old man expect?”

I believe he spoke from his heart, for his dead family and about his own passing. In truth, whenever I see an old man, or an old woman, I think on my own mortality; how I will be when I am where they are now, old and waiting to die, and I will look old and talk old. A long way off, I think, but in truth on Monday, it is closer than it was on Sunday, and sometimes I think on it and shake.

Santo tapped his knee and cleared his throat to get the old man’s attention.

“We are going to America. Have you been to America, signuri?” The old man plain ignored him; Santo asked again, and this was agitating to the old man, and he gives Santo a dirty look. He raised himself slowly off the rock, grunting the pain, finally standing stiff and straight at attention, and he had the look of some Don, except he rode a rickety and diseased mule and dressed like a beggar and was frail and ready to die. I respected his looks, in a way, as a man with years and wisdom. This is how I was taught. .

“May I present myself?” he said with pride, head high, his body stiff. “I am Giovanni Nacello, the son of Giuseppe Nacello and *Angelina Campi, both born in Sicilia, now deceased and residing with God Almighty. I am certain of it.” He wiped a thin hand across his ragged sleeve and shook my hand and Luca’s hand, saying, “grazie,” each time, for what reason I could not tell. When he came to Santo’s extended hand, the old man stared through him with the most piercing look I have seen. When Santo asked again, “have you been to America?” Signuri Nacello turned away and covered his ears.

“I heard you the first time,” he said, with an angry voice, and a twisted face. “You are an annoying man,” and he wiped the back of his neck.

“But I will answer, not that my business is your business.” He looked hard at Santo and shook his head. “I have not been to America, or any other place except Patti, where I was born and Aci Catena, where I will die.” He scratched at the stubble on his cheek and his eyes became brighten, and he looked straight on at Santo, “but I would have liked to visit the moon. But it is too late for my dreams such as this.” Santo nodded and smirked.

“The moon? My God. What would you do on the moon?”

“See the man,” he answered, pointing at the cloudless sky. “The old man up there. You have seen him, I am certain. The old man in the moon. I follow him each night he comes out…you know he is watching us. I would like to meet him and listen to his stories. Imagine what he has seen from his perch in the heavens.”

Santo laughed. “You are long in the tooth to be talking like a child.”

The old man searched the heavens and sighed, disappointed, as there was no moon to be seen, and it might not be out tonight. “What he sees from up there,” he said again and sighed again.

“Never mind up there,” Luca said, pointing to the ground. “Down here is all we care about. What have you seen down here, you and this foul mule?”

The old man swore he “rode with Garibaldi,” saying it as proudly as other men who said it before him. Another one, my God. Was there ten million men in Garibaldi’s Army? I thought, from this date on, whenever I meet any Siciliani, I will start with “and when did you ride with Garibaldi,” and if they say “never” I will consider him an honest man. But for this man I had confused him, so it seemed. “You mean on the road? If you mean on the road, then, well, I saw nothing important, except those bastard dogs. Nothing I care about, that is. Nothing worth remembering, except…,” he paused and shook his head a few times. “Si. Now I remember. In Messina, it was hell all around, very exciting. Not what I thought it would be. Violence and commotion like I have not seen before.” He brushes the shit and the dirt from his shirt, that was marked with holes and threadbare cloth.

“Men were rioting all over hell…exciting to see.”

I ask the old man, shaking my fist near his face, “You saw rioting?”

Luca’s ears perked, like a dog who sees birds to flush in the woods. There is a violent sound to the word “riot,” and this feel of death in the air when it’s said. Luca moved close to the old man and took him by the arm and shook him, but not too violent.

“You saw rioting?” spraying spit onto the old man, who shielded himself with his thin arm, that trembled more than before.

“Never turn away when I am talking to you,” Luca says. “Tell me what you saw and tell me now.” The old man tried ignoring Luca, even after his show of temper, that was real, and he slowly backed onto his rock, and leaned back, bracing his body against two wasted arms, complaining he feared his old bones would break into small pieces. His look was shy, like a cat, and there was a story he wanted to tell, but he would not be pushed or threatened, and would tell it in his own time. He talked on about his brother and the farm, and this agitated Luca, who paced, starring holes through the old man each time he passed him on the rock.

“After my brother passed, God rest his soul,” and he made the sign of the cross, “I stayed on and visited with his wife a few days...to help her clean up around the farm.” He wiped his forehead dry, sighed, and fanned himself with a trembling hand. “The goddamned heat bothers me,” he said, his voice weak, “more than when I was a young man.” It was hot, more humid hot than we expect for this time of year, but not hell hot, as papa calls the days you cannot breathe, like August. It was his age, I thought; he is at the end and cannot tolerate discomfort, as younger men do. “Before I left the farm, Assunta, my dead brother’s wife, gives me this wreck of a mule and I named him Peppino. It had no name, as I already said. ‘When the mule gives out,’ she pleaded, ‘turn it loose. Do not kill it, as it served your brother well,’ but she did give permission to kill it for meat, only if I was starving. A mule is a stupid creation, good for pulling and carrying and shitting, and I have no special love for it.”

Luca paced, mumbling to himself. “Rioting,” he kept saying. “Rioting.”

“Do you men have water?” the old man asks, but not for his mule; he would not expect us to give up water for a dumb animal, especially a mule. Maybe a regal dog, or something like that. The old man shakes his finger. “No, signuri, Peppino is on his own, and when he is thirsty enough, he will drink piss water from the ditch, if need be…. can you spare a little wine for me?”

“No wine,” I said, hard at him, but not rude. “But” and I thought twice before saying it, “we have a little water…very little to spare if you can help us out.” He nods, yes, he agreed. I give him a small taste from my flask. Only a short drink, I said, and he sipped more, slowly, as if enjoying fine Sicilian wine. At the end, he ran his tongue over and around his lips. I grabbed the flask as he went for another taste. “Just one more swig,” he asked, but he was thankful for what little I let him have, saying the others had not treated him near so well.

“You were mistreated along the way?” I asked.

“Mistreated?” He thought; he starred away and shook his head. “Mistreated, you asked me? No. Not mistreated like you mean it, but ignored, that is far worse. Worse than a good crack on the skull. Who has time for an old man?” Luca disappeared into a thicket; to piss, I figured.

He come back, all smiles, talking on the little pleasures in life. Pissing. I have not thought of it as a pleasure, but neither am I Luca.

Luca asked him again, now calmly, “You say you saw rioting in Messina?”

“I believe I might have said it.”

“Tell us the story if you saw it.”

“You want to know what I saw?” He scratched at his face. “Si. Of course, I can oblige you, but I am a little dry…let’s see. Where was I? Si, I remember now. I left my brothers farm early in the morning, to make my way home, and…” Santo interrupted him.

Giovanni stared holes through both of us. “You are strange men. And your other friend is strange. Also, he is an annoying man.” He meant Santo.

The old man coughed and doubled over, til he near threw up. He recovered and apologized, then out of thin air he complained he was tired of us and would move on, asking for one last favor, a leg up on his mule. I helped him but holding my breath. As soon as he settled on his mule, Peppino, he kicked and started North, up the Messina Road, but after a few hundred feet he caught his error, turned around and headed South. He left with no ceremony, or goodbye, or did he thank us for the bread and cheese and the wine (what did he know?) I say good riddance, as I figure he was nothing; no hinderance, or other pain in the ass, but a small inconvenience. I remember *Signuri Nacello as an odd little man, who I would forget before he was out of sight. But the truth is, I never forgot the sonofabitch and thought about him many times, always wondering if he made it home, and how long did he live and did he die painfully.

Luca, and this I could not believe, gave a finger to the old man as he rode away.

“The old fool was not worth listening to,” he said. “He barely remembers yesterday. I pay no attention to him or his silly stories. Old man or not, I should have kicked is ass to Aci Catena.”

“But he raised his hand to God,” Santo said. “It is the same as talking to God, Himself, to his face. I am certain he did not lie. Some of what he said must be true.”
