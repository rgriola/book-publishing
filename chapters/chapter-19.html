It was clear, then, that working for a wage was an insult to God; a waste of life, and not what the Almighty intended. At night, when everyone slept except other hermits (he counted ten), Alfonso walked the empty corridors to pass the time until he fell asleep. “I wonder, and Santo and Luca, they also are wondering, why did you leave Italia?”

Enzo nodded, that I should go on.

“We are contadini. We beg for work, but you, you are a teacher. We all know that educated men do not starve in Italia. You are going to a foreign country on a boat filled with men and women who have nowhere else to go. What is the sense of it?”

He shuffled in his seat and cleared his throat.

“So,” he paused. “You are thinking, what is the dark secret this man hides. Is that it?”

I nod, as we stare each other down.

“I recall said you were tired of teaching and this and that.”

“I was no longer needed…as a teacher. That is all I meant.”

“Teachers are rare in Italia, and there are few in Sicilia. It is hard to believe you cannot find a decent job.”

Enzo wiped his on the sleeves of the ragged jacket he wore. He fumbled with a button on his collar, declaring he would buy a new shirt in America. He leaned forward in his chair and placed both hands on the tabletop, as if holding it down; he looked at me for a long while, and I knew there was something he had to say. He was tired and old looking, years older than his age. I saw a troubled man, and heard it in his voice when he talked, the trembling sound, the words that were forced from his mouth. Maybe he would confess to exciting things, or he was another capu on the run, like the old man, one step ahead of the law, or worse, the brigands.

“Is it the law?” I said, standing straight. “Are you running from the Carabinieri.”

He shook his head. “No. I am sorry to disappoint you, but no one is looking for me. Who would waste time looking for an old man, a worn-out teacher of dead languages? No person of sound mind.”

“Then tell me, Enzo. Your secret? What is it?” There was a long and silent stare, but he blinked and broke and looked away, then swept his hand across the table, brushing away the crumbs for other men meals, and his cards fell onto the floor. He is too old to chase young women, too frightened to chase the married ones.

Enzo was sweating like a farm hand in the August sun.

I wanted to give him an honorable way out of this difficulty.

“Nothing,” he said. “This is what I have done with my life. I have wasted all that time and have very little left.” He looked at his hands and began to twiddle his thumbs. I have never seen a man twiddle his thumbs. My grandmother twiddled her thumbs when she lost her mind, or I have seen old women, in the piazza, twiddling their thumbs. Not men.

“But as to you and your friends, Santo and Luca, I am thankful you befriended me and allowed me to share your trip. To be honest, I was afraid to travel alone, and when we met at the dock in Napoli, I was ready to give up and leave for some other place. Any place. You said I am an educated man, but that is only one part of me, the good part and I am proud of it, that I have earned it in my own way. But we are all like the moon, with a dark side and I have mine. You see, Antonino, I have a fatal flaw from which I may not recover. I am a gambler and worse, a bad gambler. I cannot recall the last time I won.”

He expected I would be shocked at what he said, but I have known gamblers before, in the army, and they did not seem a special kind of evil men. Most of them were, well, shabby and worthless, but not evil. I smiled and he turned a little white.

I told him, “Now I understand. You are running from men who you owe money to. The hell with them. They are all vita bassa and not to be taken seriously. Pretty harmless, I guess.”

Enzo agreed.

“Yes, gamblers are *vita bassa, low life, and you are correct, most are harmless, but not all.” He stared at the floor. “And I accept I am vita bassa, too. I have fought it for years, but now I accept it.” He kept rubbing his hands together, then scratching at himself, his neck and cheek and forehead, and the sore on his chin, that on closer look, I saw was festering. “I am more than a gambler, though. I am a thief, a common thief and this is the root of my problem and, well, why I am here with you. I am also a common thief, no more, no less.” For the first time I notice his eyes were black and sunken, as if sentence his sentence had been pronounced and it was death.

He opened to me, his soul, and confessed his sins, and I was honored, but why me? What could I offer to ease his burden? Was I some priest?

“You? A thief? No. I cannot believe it. Not a man with your education and manner. You are making this up to make yourself seem…what is it...to make yourself seem more masculine, man of the world.” Then I thought, who would call himself a thief when he was not a thief? I was certain he had a good reason to steal, if he did.

“You stole what you needed to survive…like food. Correct, and not out of greed. This I can understand. You were you down on your luck? How is it a crime?” He lay his head on the table for a long time, and I could hear him talking, but not to me, and I figure he was praying. I would have worn out my knees, praying, if I were him. Finally, he raised himself straight in his chair, looked me in the eyes and sighed and breathed deep.

“To answer your question,” he paused, then licked his lips, “and it is an honest question. No. I was not starving.” He brushed his hand across the table, made a nervous fidget, and picked at the sore on his chin. At that moment, he was anything but a teacher, as I would guess they are all neat and clean and respectable, and his clothes were filthy and ragged, not even a good wash by a good woman could save them. Out of the blue, with no expression, he said this, plainly, “I stole from my students.” I stared, and immediately, I doubted what he said.

He cleared his throat. “For years, may God forgive me,” and he makes a perfect sign of the cross, “Gambling was a way of life for me, like some men play chess, or read, or watch the ocean. Now I am no better than a common roustabout. The last time I stole, it was from a student, and I was caught, like the fool I am, and the headmaster took joy in firing me, and blackballing me for the rest of my life.” He paused and brushed at his jacket.

“So now I am here, with you and your friends. God help you all.”

I was shocked, yes, but I believed I could still like this man, but I could not, in my head, believe that what he did was all right. It was not. And I was angry.

“You stole from children? Is that what you are saying?” He stared at the floor.

I could not put these words together with any sense. ‘Thief,’ and ‘steal,’ and ‘children.’ He moved his chair next to mine and leaned in close, his face inches away, and I felt his breath when he talked. I was too close to another man, and I pushed him back.

“This last boy was my Waterloo. His father was a great landowner from near Napoli; wealthy and powerful, unforgiving to men like me. These men send their sons to the great cities, where they associate with the best and are educated by the best of teachers. Their families do not countenance men who steal from their children, but it was not the devil who was my enemy, so much as boredom. There are just so many books to read and lectures to hear, and you spend too much time with yourself. I succumbed to the spell of Lady Luck. Gambling. I was captivated by her wiles, and the thrill of it, like nothing I have felt before. I had little money to bet, and usually I would lose it all. So, I…”

“You stole from children because you were bored? Is that what…”

Two men, younger than me, and tall, asked if they could they sit down, as there were not two other empty chairs in the room. I lied. “Mi dispiaci. I am sorry, but these chairs belong to our women when they return.” They accepted my lie (a harmless lie) and left with no fuss or complaint. Enzo said I should not have lied to the men, that we should have welcomed them or moved, then he apologized for ‘moralizing,’ that he had no right. The strain was showing on his grey face, the color of a corpse, but when I touched his arm, he roused a little and gave a weak smile.

“Did I steal because I was bored? No. Not that. Boredom had nothing to do with my vice. I drank most nights, at a bar near the school where I taught. There were, well, there were women employees, and they…” He stared off. “You understand what I mean. I believe you were in the army.”

I knew what he meant, but I was embarrassed to say I did.

“Si. Those women. I understand what you say.” He was speaking of prostitutes and whores, but I was too embarrassed to say what everyman knows.

“They were good looking women,” he said, with a faint smile. “Not beautiful, but fair as women go. And I admit, for a glass of decent wine and a few lire, well, they would sit and talk and sometimes they…well, you know. Have you been lonely?” He stared at me, waiting, his mouth open, and I realized he asked a question, expecting my answer.

“Si, who has not been lonely?” He nodded and smiled, and I believe he was thinking back to her.

“There was this one special lady, Leticia, half my age and she could curse like a man. I found it seductive, uh, all of it, but in the end I God-damned her soul to hell.” He paused and cleared his throat. “I know it was evil, what I did, and I should not have done it, but she used her wiles and did me in. I was shamed me into a card game with four men, younger than me and better card players. I should have refused, but I would have seemed old and weak to this younger woman. I wanted to impress her so she would stay around, and, maybe, she would...” He took a deep breath.

A camp follower? I have seen them before, these camp followers, but not a beautiful camp follower, not even good looking, or attractive, but they all swore like soldiers and some men found it sweet and appealing, like some music, or good wine. I have not heard a woman curse in a long time; my grandmother, sometimes, but she is sick and dying.

“I was drunk,” he said, “Easily cheated. I lost more than I could pay. Her bastard friends threatened to break my legs with a rock, no matter how long it took, or how much I screamed. ‘Who will hear you in the woods?’ is what they said and laughed. ‘The trees?’

“Then you robbed a young boy?” I ask. He was annoyed the way I put the question, and he pushed his chair from the table and half stood up.

“Robbery?” He stepped away from the table. “My God, no. What do you think I am? Robbery is a crime of violence. There was no violence. Am I a violent man? A theft. A petty theft, that’s all it was. I swear.”

“A petty theft, that’s all,” he kept repeating.

“My plan was simple. I surprised my students with an examination on Latin verbs. Even the best student would need a full hour to finish. They complained when I announced the test, but I reminded them ‘from adversity comes success.’”

“Yes, adversity. It means, uh, something like pain and suffering.”

“Yes. You learn fast, Antonino. And to make it easy, I invoked the honor system, reminding them of severe penalties for cheating, and then I left the room.”

Yes, of course they understood the special problem women have, as they were both married men. They apologized for bothering us and wandered away. Enzo continued his story. .

“This rich little bastard, *Donato, always hung his jacket on the same hook outside my classroom. If another boy hung his jacket on Donato’s hook, it would end up on the floor. He took a rag off the table and wiped his forehead and the back of his neck, then folded it and smoothed it and placed it back in the same spot. Neat and civilized, of course.

“I knew my word would count little against the son of a wealthy man; I refused to protest or argue, to belittle myself. In the end, you must believe this, I was too honest to lie. Another Ironia?”

This word he used, ‘ironia,’ was not a word I knew, but from what he said about his ordeal, I am proud to say I figured it out. I believed I understood its meaning. But no, I did not see the ironia.

“Did they call the cops?”

“If you mean the Carabinieri, no, they were not called, thank God. The headmaster did not want it known there was a thief in his employ, who taught the sons of the rich and powerful in Roma. “Soon you will be with your brother and his is family,” I said only to calm him.

“Well, yes. My brother is somewhere in America. I have heard from men who knew him, but we have not seen each other or written for many years. I will find him, though, if he is still alive.”

“It is north of Roma, but south of Genova. The weather is fine and temperate.”

“No degree. Gone. Lost in a card game, to a cavone who can barely speaks his own language. He wanted it to brag to his friends. I believe he cheated me. I will never know the truth.”

Men should not cry, no matter the pain. Women may cry, it is their right as women, but men, never. I have seen men cry, and I am embarrassed for them.
